# Letters on the Strength of Things

### A Treatise on Forces, Materials, and Structures, from the Lever to the Bridge

*In the manner of Euler's Letters to a German Princess*

---

## Preface

Every structure is a conversation between force and material. The stone wall speaks to gravity. The timber beam negotiates with the load upon it. The arch redirects what would crush it into a thrust it can bear. The builder who understands this conversation builds what endures. The builder who does not is at the mercy of accident.

I write these letters because the African continent is building. From Lagos to Kigali, from Addis Ababa to Dar es Salaam, structures rise daily — houses, schools, bridges, dams. Many are built by hands that know their craft through tradition and apprenticeship, which is a noble and ancient form of knowledge. But tradition alone cannot explain why a beam of a certain depth will carry a classroom of children safely while one slightly thinner will not. Tradition alone cannot tell you when a column will buckle, or why a triangle holds rigid while a rectangle collapses, or how much steel to place inside a concrete beam so that it bends gracefully instead of snapping without warning.

These letters aim to provide that understanding. They proceed as Euler proceeded in his famous letters to a German princess — from first principles, in plain language, with patience. We begin with the nature of force itself, move through simple machines and the behavior of materials, then build upward through beams, columns, trusses, arches, and domes, until at last we can trace the path of every load from rooftop to earth and understand why each element along that path must be sized as it is.

I have drawn examples from African building wherever possible: the dry-stone walls of Great Zimbabwe, which have stood for seven centuries without mortar; the rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, carved from living basalt; the Nubian vault technique of the upper Nile, which builds curved roofs without formwork; the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, which harnesses the Blue Nile. These are not decorations added to a foreign science. They are structural engineering, practiced on African soil, sometimes centuries before European theory caught up to explain why they worked.

The mathematics in these letters is arithmetic and simple algebra — nothing beyond what a secondary school provides. Where I use a formula, I use it because the formula is the shortest and most honest way to express a truth. A builder who can multiply force by distance and divide by a safety factor holds more power than one who memorizes tables without understanding them.

Let us begin, then, with the simplest question: what is a force?

## Part I: The Nature of Force

### Letter 1: On Force and the Push That Shapes the World

My dear reader, before we can speak of beams and bridges, of arches and foundations, we must settle the most elementary question of all: what is a force? For everything in structural engineering — every calculation, every design choice, every failure and every success — begins with forces and how they act upon matter.

A force is simply a push or a pull. When you press your hand against a wall, you exert a force upon it. When you lift a bucket of water from a well, you exert an upward force upon the rope. When the wind blows against the side of a building, it exerts a horizontal force upon the wall. Forces are invisible, but their effects are plain: they accelerate things, they deform things, they hold things in place or send them tumbling.

Sir Isaac Newton, the great English natural philosopher, gave us the first law of motion, which is the foundation upon which all structural reasoning rests. The law states this: a body at rest remains at rest, and a body in motion remains in motion at constant speed in a straight line, unless acted upon by a force. Consider a stone lying on the ground in the Sahel. It does not move. It has sat there for a thousand years and will sit there for a thousand more, unless something pushes it — a foot, a flood, an earthquake. This is not because the stone is "naturally still." It is because no unbalanced force acts upon it. The moment you kick it, you apply a force, and it moves. The moment you stop kicking, friction (another force, which we shall meet later) brings it to rest again.

Now here is the insight that matters for the builder: a structure that is standing still is a body at rest. Newton's first law tells us that every force acting upon it must be balanced by an equal and opposite force. The roof pushes down on the wall; the wall pushes up on the roof. The wall pushes down on the foundation; the foundation pushes up on the wall. The foundation pushes down on the earth; the earth pushes up on the foundation. If at any point in this chain a force goes unbalanced — if the earth cannot push back hard enough, or the wall is too weak to transmit the load — the structure moves. And when a structure moves, we call that collapse.

This is why I say that every structure is a conversation between force and material. The load says: I push. The material says: I resist. As long as the material's resistance equals or exceeds the load's push, the conversation is polite and the building stands. The moment the load exceeds the resistance, the conversation ends badly. Every letter that follows is an exploration of this conversation — how to listen to it, how to predict it, and how to ensure that the material always has the last word.

Forces are measured in Newtons, named after the man who first described them precisely. One Newton is roughly the force exerted by gravity on a small apple — which is fitting, given the old story. A kilonewton (kN) is a thousand Newtons, roughly the weight of a hundred kilograms. When we speak of building loads, we speak in kilonewtons, because buildings are heavy and the forces they must resist are large. A single concrete floor slab for a classroom might weigh 50 kN. The students and furniture upon it might add another 15 kN. The structure must carry all of it, continuously, for decades, without complaint.

### Letter 2: On Newton's Second Law and the Equation of Motion

Having established that a force is a push or a pull, and that a body at rest remains at rest unless a force disturbs it, let us now consider what happens when a force does act. Newton's second law tells us: the force acting on a body equals the mass of the body multiplied by its acceleration. In symbols, F = ma. This is perhaps the most important equation in all of physics, and it is certainly the equation upon which all of structural engineering is built.

Let us parse it carefully. Mass is the quantity of matter in a body. A large block of granite quarried from the hills near Abeokuta has more mass than a small one. Mass is measured in kilograms. Acceleration is the rate at which velocity changes — how quickly something speeds up or slows down, measured in meters per second per second. Force, then, is mass times acceleration. If you wish to accelerate a 1000-kilogram ox cart at 1 meter per second per second, you need a force of 1000 Newtons. If you wish to accelerate it at 2 meters per second per second, you need 2000 Newtons. Twice the acceleration demands twice the force. And if you have two ox carts, each of 1000 kilograms, and wish to accelerate them both at 1 meter per second per second, you need 2000 Newtons — twice the mass demands twice the force.

This is why heavier things are harder to move. It is not merely a matter of common sense; it is a precise mathematical relationship. The farmer in the highlands of Ethiopia who uses two oxen to pull a heavily loaded cart instead of one is not merely being cautious — he is obeying F = ma. The load is large (large m), the desired acceleration is modest (small a), but the product is still substantial, and a single ox cannot provide it.

For the structural engineer, F = ma matters most in a special case: when acceleration is zero. If a building is standing still, its acceleration is zero. F = ma then tells us that the net force on the building is zero. This does not mean that no forces act — gravity pulls the building down with enormous force. It means that the sum of all forces must be zero: the downward pull of gravity is exactly balanced by the upward push of the foundation and the earth beneath it. This condition — net force equals zero — is called static equilibrium, and it is the central requirement of every structure that does not move.

There is a second condition for equilibrium that we must mention here, though we shall develop it more fully later: the sum of all moments (turning effects) about any point must also be zero. A seesaw balanced at its center is in equilibrium not only because the forces balance (equal weights on each side) but because the turning effects balance (equal weights at equal distances). A building that satisfies force equilibrium but not moment equilibrium will topple over, as many a poorly designed retaining wall has demonstrated.

F = ma also governs dynamic situations — earthquakes, wind gusts, the sudden impact of a truck on a bridge. In these cases, acceleration is emphatically not zero, and the forces generated can be enormous. A magnitude 7 earthquake can produce ground accelerations of 3 to 5 meters per second per second. Applied to a building massing hundreds of thousands of kilograms, the resulting forces shake walls apart, snap columns, and heave foundations from the ground. We shall address earthquake resistance in a later letter, but the principle is already clear: F = ma, and when a is large, F is terrifying.

For now, let us hold onto the central lesson. Force equals mass times acceleration. For structures at rest, net force is zero and net moment is zero. These two conditions — force equilibrium and moment equilibrium — are the twin tests that every structure must pass at every instant of its existence. If either test fails, the structure moves, and movement in a structure is another name for failure.

### Letter 3: On Newton's Third Law and the Wall That Pushes Back

Newton's third law is the most misunderstood and the most important of the three for the builder. It states: for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. When you push against a wall with a force of 100 Newtons, the wall pushes back against your hand with a force of 100 Newtons. When a 10,000-kilogram roof bears down on a column with a force of 98,000 Newtons, the column pushes up on the roof with a force of 98,000 Newtons. The action and the reaction are always equal, always opposite, and they act on different bodies.

This is not merely a curiosity. It is the foundation of every structure ever built. Consider the simplest possible structure: a stone block sitting on the ground. Gravity pulls the block downward with a force equal to its weight. The ground pushes the block upward with an equal force. The block does not accelerate, because the two forces cancel. But notice: the ground must push. If the ground cannot push back — if it is too soft, too wet, too weak — the stone sinks. This is why buildings on poor soil require deep foundations or piles driven to firmer strata below. The soil must be able to push back with a force equal to the building's weight, or the building sinks.

Now extend the principle to every joint in a structure. Where a beam meets a column, the beam pushes down on the column (it carries load), and the column pushes up on the beam (it supports the load). Where a column meets a foundation, the column pushes down and the foundation pushes up. Where a foundation meets the earth, the foundation pushes down and the earth pushes up. At every connection, Newton's third law is at work. The entire structure is a chain of action-reaction pairs, from the roof tiles at the top to the bedrock at the bottom.

The African mason building a mud-brick wall in the Sahel understands this intuitively. He knows that the wall must rest on firm ground, that each course of brick must bear the weight of the courses above, and that the lowest course bears the most weight and must therefore be the strongest. He may not recite Newton's third law, but he builds in accordance with it. Our purpose in these letters is to make the intuitive precise, so that the builder can extend his knowledge beyond what tradition alone provides — to longer spans, heavier loads, and materials he has not used before.

There is a subtle but crucial implication of the third law that I wish to emphasize. You cannot push on something without being pushed back. This means that a structure can never simply "absorb" a force and make it disappear. Every force that enters a structure must exit it. The wind pushes on the wall; the wall transmits the force to the foundation; the foundation transmits it to the earth. If any element in the chain cannot transmit the force — if the connection between wall and foundation is too weak, for instance — the chain breaks, and the structure fails at that point. The art of structural engineering is, in large measure, the art of ensuring that every force has a continuous, unbroken path from where it enters the structure to where it exits into the ground.

We call this concept the load path, and it is so important that we shall devote an entire letter to it later. For now, remember the third law: every push has a pushback, and a structure is nothing more than an organized system of pushes and pushbacks, all the way from sky to soil.

### Letter 4: On Gravity and the Force That Holds Us Down

Of all the forces that act upon structures, gravity is the most constant and the most inescapable. The wind comes and goes. Earthquakes strike rarely. But gravity pulls downward on every atom of every structure, every second of every day, without pause and without mercy. The primary job of any structure is to resist gravity — to hold things up that would otherwise fall down.

The force of gravity on a body is called its weight, and it is calculated simply: weight equals mass times the acceleration due to gravity. The acceleration due to gravity at the Earth's surface is approximately 9.81 meters per second per second, which we round to 10 for quick calculation. A block of concrete with a mass of 100 kilograms therefore weighs approximately 1000 Newtons, or 1 kilonewton. A person with a mass of 70 kilograms weighs about 700 Newtons. These are the forces that structures must carry.

Gravity is what makes the Nile flow from the highlands of Ethiopia northward to the Mediterranean. The river does not choose to flow north; it flows downhill, which happens to be northward in the Nile's case. The same principle governs rainwater on a roof: it flows downhill, following the slope, and the builder must ensure that the slope directs it away from the building rather than into it. Gravity is not merely a structural force; it is the force that governs drainage, erosion, and the stability of hillside construction.

For the builder, gravity creates two kinds of load. The first is the dead load — the weight of the structure itself. The concrete slab weighs so much per square meter. The steel beams weigh so much per meter of length. The roof tiles, the walls, the foundations — all have weight, and all that weight must be carried continuously. The second is the live load — the weight of everything that is not the structure itself but rests upon it. People, furniture, stored goods, vehicles on a bridge. Live loads come and go, but the structure must be ready for them at all times.

Consider a simple example: a reinforced concrete floor slab in a school in Kigali. The slab is 150 millimeters thick and spans 4 meters. Reinforced concrete weighs approximately 25 kilonewtons per cubic meter. The dead load of the slab is therefore 0.15 meters times 25 kN/m³ = 3.75 kN per square meter. The live load for a classroom — students, desks, the teacher pacing — is typically taken as 3.0 kN per square meter. The total load is 6.75 kN per square meter. Over a 4-meter by 6-meter classroom, that is 162 kN — roughly the weight of 16 tonnes pressing down on the beams and walls below. The structure must carry this without excessive deflection or distress, day after day, for the life of the building.

Gravity also creates a phenomenon called overturning. A tall, narrow structure — a chimney, a water tower, a tall retaining wall — can be toppled by a horizontal force such as wind. Gravity resists this toppling: the weight of the structure acts downward through its center, creating a stabilizing moment. But if the wind moment exceeds the gravity moment, the structure overturns. This is why tall structures must be either heavy enough or wide enough at their base to resist overturning. The pyramids of Egypt, with their enormous weight and broad bases, are perhaps the most over-engineered structures in history against overturning — which is one reason they have stood for four and a half millennia.

### Letter 5: On Friction and the Grip That Prevents Sliding

Friction is the force that resists the sliding of one surface over another. Without it, you could not walk (your feet would slip on the ground), you could not drive a nail (it would slide out of the wood), and you could not build a dry-stone wall (the stones would slide apart). Friction is the unsung hero of construction, and the builder who understands it builds with confidence.

There are two kinds of friction relevant to the builder. Static friction is the force that prevents two surfaces from starting to slide against each other. Kinetic friction is the force that resists motion once sliding has begun. Static friction is always greater than kinetic friction. This is why it takes more force to start pushing a heavy crate across a floor than to keep it moving once it has started. It is also why a structure that begins to slide — a retaining wall, for instance, under pressure from the earth behind it — accelerates once it starts moving, because the resisting friction drops from static to kinetic.

The magnitude of friction depends on two things: the force pressing the two surfaces together (called the normal force), and a property of the surfaces called the coefficient of friction. Friction equals the coefficient of friction times the normal force. The coefficient of friction is a number, typically between 0.1 and 1.0, that depends on the materials. Rubber on concrete has a high coefficient — about 0.7 — which is why tires grip roads. Steel on steel has a lower coefficient — about 0.3 — which is why railways are efficient but wheels slip on steep grades. Wood on wood varies from about 0.3 to 0.5, depending on the species and whether the surfaces are smooth or rough.

The Great Enclosure at Great Zimbabwe provides a magnificent example of friction at work. The walls of the Great Enclosure are built of carefully shaped granite blocks, stacked without mortar, rising to a height of 11 meters. What holds them together? Friction. The weight of the upper courses presses down on the lower courses, creating an enormous normal force. The rough granite surfaces provide a respectable coefficient of friction. The product — friction force — is large enough to prevent the stones from sliding, even under wind load, even through centuries of thermal expansion and contraction. The builders of Great Zimbabwe did not know the word "friction," but they understood its principle perfectly: heavy stones with rough surfaces, carefully fitted, will hold.

Nails and screws rely on friction as well. When you drive a nail into wood, the wood fibers press against the nail's shank, creating friction that resists withdrawal. A longer nail has more surface area in contact with the wood and therefore more friction — which is why longer nails hold better. A screw has threads that interlock with the wood, but even a screw relies partly on friction between its threads and the surrounding material. The builder who understands this knows why a nail driven into end grain (parallel to the fibers) holds poorly — the fibers do not press tightly against the shank — while a nail driven across the grain holds well.

Friction also governs the stability of foundations. A spread foundation on soil resists horizontal forces through friction between its base and the soil beneath it. If the horizontal force (from wind, from earth pressure, from an earthquake) exceeds the friction capacity of the foundation, the foundation slides. The friction capacity equals the coefficient of friction between concrete and soil (typically 0.3 to 0.5) times the vertical load on the foundation. This is why heavier buildings resist sliding better than lighter ones, and why foundations for buildings in seismic zones are sometimes keyed into the soil — a projection on the bottom of the foundation that digs into the earth and provides resistance beyond friction alone.

## Part II: Simple Machines

### Letter 6: On the Lever and the Multiplication of Effort

Archimedes of Syracuse declared: "Give me a place to stand, and I shall move the Earth." He was speaking of the lever, the simplest and most ancient of machines, and he was not entirely exaggerating. The lever is a rigid bar that rotates about a fixed point called the fulcrum, and it allows a small force applied over a large distance to produce a large force over a small distance. This is mechanical advantage, and it is the principle behind every pry bar, every wheelbarrow, and every well sweep on the continent.

The law of the lever is this: force times distance on one side of the fulcrum equals force times distance on the other side. If you place the fulcrum close to a heavy stone and push down on the long end of the bar, you can lift a stone many times your own weight. The ratio of the distances — the length of your arm of the lever to the length of the stone's arm — is the mechanical advantage. A lever arm five times as long as the resistance arm gives a mechanical advantage of five: you push with 100 Newtons and lift 500 Newtons.

The African well sweep — known in Egypt as the shaduf and used along the Nile for four thousand years — is a lever in action. A long pole is balanced on a fulcrum, with a bucket on one end and a counterweight of mud or stone on the other. The counterweight nearly balances the weight of a full bucket of water, so the farmer needs only a small additional force to lift the water from the well. The shaduf allows one person to irrigate fields that would otherwise require two or three, not by adding strength but by applying the principle of the lever.

There are three classes of lever, distinguished by the arrangement of the fulcrum, the effort, and the load. In a first-class lever, the fulcrum is between the effort and the load — like a seesaw or a pry bar. In a second-class lever, the load is between the fulcrum and the effort — like a wheelbarrow, where the wheel is the fulcrum, the load is in the tray, and you lift at the handles. In a third-class lever, the effort is between the fulcrum and the load — like a pair of tongs, where the hinge is the fulcrum, your hand squeezes in the middle, and the tips grip the object. Third-class levers have a mechanical advantage less than one — you must apply more force than the load — but they give you greater speed and range of motion.

The structural engineer uses the lever principle constantly, though often without calling it by name. When a beam bends under load, the internal forces act as a lever: compression in the top, tension in the bottom, with the neutral axis as the fulcrum. When a retaining wall resists earth pressure, the wall acts as a lever: the earth pushes on one side, the wall's weight pushes on the other, and the toe of the foundation is the fulcrum. When a crane lifts a load, the boom is a lever, the base is the fulcrum, and the counterweight on the far side balances the load. Every moment calculation in structural engineering is, at bottom, a lever calculation.

Let me leave you with a practical observation. The lever tells us that the position of a force matters as much as its magnitude. A 10 kN force applied 5 meters from a fulcrum creates the same turning effect as a 50 kN force applied 1 meter from the fulcrum. This is why a long-handled wrench loosens a stubborn bolt that a short one cannot budge. It is why a wide foundation resists overturning better than a narrow one. And it is why, in structural design, the location of every force must be specified as precisely as its magnitude. Force times distance — the moment — is the quantity that governs bending, overturning, and stability. We shall meet it again and again.

### Letter 7: On the Pulley and the Rope That Lifts

A pulley is a wheel with a groove around its rim through which a rope passes. In its simplest form — a single fixed pulley — it provides no mechanical advantage at all. You pull down on one end of the rope with a force equal to the weight you wish to lift. The pulley merely changes the direction of force, allowing you to pull downward (using your body weight) to lift something upward. This is already useful: it is easier and safer to stand on the ground and pull a rope downward than to climb to the top of a scaffold and haul a bucket upward by hand.

But the true power of the pulley appears when pulleys are combined. A single movable pulley — one that moves with the load — gives a mechanical advantage of two. The load is supported by two segments of rope, each carrying half the weight, so you need to pull with only half the force. Add another pulley, and the advantage increases. A block and tackle with four rope segments gives a mechanical advantage of four: a worker pulling with 250 Newtons can lift 1000 Newtons, which is roughly 100 kilograms.

On construction sites across Africa, from the high-rises of Lagos to the dam projects of the Zambezi, the pulley system is at work every day. The tower crane that lifts concrete skips to the twentieth floor is an elaborate system of pulleys and cables, but the principle is the same as a boy lifting a bucket from a well with a rope over a branch. The crane's advantage comes from multiple sheaves (pulleys) in its hook block, combined with the mechanical advantage of its boom (which acts as a lever). A modern tower crane can lift 10 tonnes to a height of 50 meters, but the motor driving it need only apply a fraction of that force, thanks to the compounded advantage of its pulley system.

There is, of course, a price. The law of conservation of energy — which we shall discuss in Letter 10 — demands it. If you gain mechanical advantage, you lose distance. With a mechanical advantage of four, you must pull four meters of rope to lift the load one meter. The total work (force times distance) is the same. You cannot cheat physics; you can only redistribute effort in a way that suits your body and your tools. A person can pull a rope all day with moderate force, but cannot exert a huge force even briefly. The pulley system trades distance for force, matching the machine to the human.

The pulley teaches a lesson that recurs throughout structural engineering: force can be redirected and redistributed, but never created or destroyed. A cable-stayed bridge redirects the vertical load of the deck into diagonal tension in the cables, which then passes into compression in the tower. The load is not diminished — it is merely directed along a path that the materials can efficiently resist. This is exactly what a pulley does: it redirects force along a rope to a direction that the worker can efficiently apply. The structural engineer is, in a sense, designing an elaborate system of pulleys — not literal pulleys, but paths along which forces are redirected from where they arise to where they can be resisted.

### Letter 8: On the Inclined Plane and the Ramp to the Pyramid

The inclined plane is a flat surface set at an angle to the horizontal. It is one of the oldest machines, and arguably the one that has moved the most mass in human history. Every ramp, every staircase, every road winding up a mountainside is an inclined plane. And the most famous application of the inclined plane in all of engineering is almost certainly the construction of the Egyptian pyramids.

The principle is straightforward. To lift a block of stone weighing 25 kilonewtons (about 2.5 tonnes, a typical pyramid block) straight up to a height of one meter requires a force of 25 kN applied over a distance of one meter. But to slide the same block up a ramp 10 meters long that rises one meter requires (ignoring friction) a force of only 2.5 kN applied over 10 meters. The mechanical advantage is the ratio of the ramp's length to its height — in this case, 10 to 1. The work is the same, but the force is divided by 10, at the cost of moving the block 10 times as far.

The Egyptians moved millions of blocks, some weighing 50 tonnes or more, to heights exceeding 140 meters. They did this with human labor, wooden sledges, and ramps. The exact ramp configuration is debated — straight ramps, spiral ramps, internal ramps — but the inclined plane was certainly the enabling machine. Without it, the pyramids could not have been built with the technology available. The lesson for the modern builder is the same: when you cannot lift a load directly, ramp it. Construction sites routinely use ramps for wheelbarrows, concrete buggies, and even trucks to reach elevated work platforms.

The wedge is a special case of the inclined plane — a double inclined plane, in fact. When you drive a wedge under a heavy object, the horizontal force you apply is converted into a much larger vertical force that lifts the object. An axe is a wedge: the horizontal force of the swing is converted into a splitting force that is many times greater. Chisels, knives, and nail points are all wedges. The African carpenter shaping iroko or mvule with an adze is using an inclined plane with every stroke.

The screw is another special case — an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. When you turn a screw, you move it along the inclined plane of its thread. The mechanical advantage is the ratio of the circumference of the screw's head (the distance your hand travels in one turn) to the pitch of the thread (the distance the screw advances in one turn). A screw with a head circumference of 60 millimeters and a pitch of 2 millimeters has a mechanical advantage of 30. This is why screws hold so well: the force required to pull them out must overcome this mechanical advantage, multiplied by friction. It is also why a screw jack can lift a truck with a small handle — the mechanical advantage of the screw is enormous.

Every time you encounter a slope in construction — the rake of a roof, the batter of a retaining wall, the grade of a road — you are encountering the inclined plane. The pitch of a roof determines how effectively it sheds rain (steeper is better, up to a point). The batter of a wall (the inward slope from base to top) improves its stability against overturning. The grade of a road determines how much force a vehicle needs to climb it. In each case, the geometry of the slope — length, height, and angle — governs the forces involved, and the governing principle is the inclined plane.

### Letter 9: On the Wheel and the Circle That Conquers Friction

The wheel is perhaps the most celebrated invention in human history, and its principle is elegant: replace sliding friction with rolling friction. When you drag a heavy crate across the ground, the friction between the crate and the ground resists you with a force proportional to the weight of the crate. The coefficient of sliding friction might be 0.5 — meaning you need to push with a force equal to half the crate's weight just to keep it moving. But place the crate on a cart with wheels, and the friction drops dramatically. The coefficient of rolling friction between a wheel and a hard surface might be 0.01 — fifty times less than sliding friction. The wheel does not eliminate friction, but it reduces it to a fraction of what it was.

The wheel and axle is a refinement: a large wheel fixed to a smaller axle. When you turn the wheel, the axle turns with it, but the force is multiplied by the ratio of the wheel's radius to the axle's radius. A steering wheel, a doorknob, a windlass for drawing water — all are wheel-and-axle machines. The windlass at a well in the dry lands of northern Kenya transforms a modest pull on a handle into a force sufficient to lift a heavy bucket of water from a deep bore.

Bearings are the engineering solution to the friction that remains even with wheels. A plain bearing — a hole in which a shaft rotates — has metal-on-metal friction. A roller bearing interposes rolling elements (balls or cylinders) between the shaft and the housing, reducing friction further. Modern bearings are marvels of precision manufacturing, but the principle is ancient: rolling friction is less than sliding friction, so interpose something that rolls.

The wheel changed civilization because it made transport efficient. Before the wheel, heavy loads were dragged on sledges or carried on heads and backs. With the wheel, the same load could be moved with a fraction of the effort. Roads became valuable because wheels work best on smooth, hard surfaces — rolling friction increases on soft or rough ground. This is why the Romans built paved roads, and why modern Africa's development is inseparable from its road network. A paved road in good condition has a rolling friction coefficient ten times lower than an unpaved track. The difference is not abstract; it is the difference between a truck delivering cement affordably and a truck burning so much fuel on a bad road that the cement doubles in price.

For the structural engineer, wheels appear in the form of rollers at bridge supports. A bridge must be free to expand and contract with temperature changes. If both ends are fixed, thermal expansion creates enormous forces that can crack the structure. The solution is to fix one end and place the other on rollers (or a modern equivalent, a sliding bearing), allowing the bridge to grow and shrink freely. The roller support carries vertical load but allows horizontal movement — it is a wheel used not for transport but for structural freedom. This simple device, based on the ancient principle that rolling friction is less than sliding friction, prevents the destruction of bridges around the world.

### Letter 10: On Work, Energy, and the Conservation of Effort

We have spoken of forces, and we have spoken of machines that multiply force. Now we must speak of the price of that multiplication, which brings us to the concepts of work and energy and the most important principle in all of physics: you never get something for nothing.

Work, in the physicist's precise meaning, is force multiplied by distance. If you lift a 10-kilogram block of stone (weight approximately 100 Newtons) through a height of 2 meters, you do work equal to 100 N times 2 m = 200 joules (the joule being the unit of work and energy, named after the English brewer and physicist James Prescott Joule). If you push a loaded wheelbarrow with a force of 50 Newtons for a distance of 20 meters, you do 1000 joules of work. Notice that work depends on both force and distance. A large force applied through zero distance does no work — which is why holding a heavy suitcase stationary makes your arm tired but does not, in the physicist's accounting, constitute work.

Energy is the capacity to do work. A raised stone has potential energy — by virtue of its height, it can do work as it falls. A moving truck has kinetic energy — by virtue of its motion, it can do work when it strikes something (which is why vehicle impacts on bridge piers are a serious design concern). A stretched spring has elastic energy. Fuel has chemical energy. The sun radiates energy that drives wind, rain, and the growth of the trees from which we cut timber.

The law of conservation of energy states that energy can be neither created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. When a waterfall drives a mill wheel, gravitational potential energy is converted to kinetic energy of the water, then to rotational energy of the wheel, then to mechanical energy of the grinding stones. At each step, some energy is lost to friction (converted to heat), but the total energy — useful plus wasted — is exactly conserved. The water wheel on the Blue Nile, used for centuries to grind grain, obeys this law precisely.

For the builder, conservation of energy means that no machine can multiply both force and distance simultaneously. A lever with a mechanical advantage of 5 multiplies force by 5, but you must move your end 5 times as far as the load moves. A pulley system with a mechanical advantage of 4 reduces the required force by 4, but you must pull 4 times as much rope. The work input always equals the work output (plus friction losses). This is why perpetual motion machines are impossible, and why every machine, no matter how clever, requires an energy input equal to or greater than its energy output.

This principle has a direct structural application. When a beam bends under load, the load does work on the beam (force times deflection). This work is stored in the beam as elastic strain energy — the energy of its internal deformation. If the load is removed, the beam springs back, returning the stored energy. But if the load exceeds the beam's elastic capacity, the work goes into permanent deformation — the beam yields and does not spring back. And if the load exceeds the beam's ultimate capacity, the remaining work goes into fracture — the beam breaks. The energy must go somewhere. Understanding where it goes is the key to understanding structural failure.

The Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam on the Blue Nile stores an enormous quantity of potential energy in its reservoir — billions of cubic meters of water held at elevation. This energy, when released through turbines, will generate over 6,000 megawatts of electricity. But the dam must also resist this energy. The water presses against the dam face with a force proportional to its depth, and the dam must transmit this force — through its massive concrete body, into the rock foundation, and ultimately into the Earth — without moving. The dam is, in energy terms, a device for holding gravitational potential energy in check, releasing it only through controlled channels. Every structure, in its own way, does the same: it holds forces in equilibrium and releases energy only through planned paths.

## Part III: Materials and Their Behavior

### Letter 11: On Stress and Strain and the Material Under Load

We have spoken of forces, and we have spoken of machines. Now we must speak of materials — the substances from which structures are built — and how they behave when forces are applied to them. This is the heart of structural engineering, for a force is merely an abstraction until it meets a material, and a material is merely inert until a force acts upon it. The conversation between force and material is told in the language of stress and strain.

Stress is force divided by area. If you press down on a wooden post with a force of 10,000 Newtons, and the post's cross-sectional area is 0.01 square meters (a post about 100 mm by 100 mm), then the stress in the post is 10,000 / 0.01 = 1,000,000 Pascals, or 1 megapascal (1 MPa). The pascal (Pa) is the unit of stress: one Newton per square meter. Since structural stresses are typically in the millions of pascals, we use megapascals routinely. Concrete has a compressive strength of 20 to 50 MPa. Structural steel has a yield strength of 250 to 500 MPa. Timber varies widely, from 5 to 50 MPa depending on species and direction of loading.

Strain is deformation divided by original length. If a 3-meter steel rod stretches by 1.5 millimeters under load, the strain is 0.0015 / 3 = 0.0005, or 0.05%. Strain has no units — it is a ratio, a pure number. Structural strains are typically very small, which is why buildings do not visibly deform under normal loads. But these small strains are real, and if they become too large, the structure has failed in serviceability even if it has not failed in strength.

The relationship between stress and strain is the material's story. If you apply gradually increasing stress to a material specimen and measure the resulting strain, you get a stress-strain curve. This curve is the material's autobiography. It tells you everything: how stiff the material is, when it begins to yield, how much it can deform before breaking, and how much energy it absorbs before failure. Different materials tell very different stories. Steel's curve rises steeply (it is stiff), reaches a yield plateau (it deforms without increasing stress), then rises again (strain hardening), and finally drops as it necks and fractures. Concrete's curve rises, reaches a peak, and then drops rapidly — concrete is brittle, failing suddenly with little warning. Timber's curve is complex, depending on whether the load is parallel or perpendicular to the grain.

The reason we use stress rather than force, and strain rather than deformation, is universality. A force of 10,000 Newtons might crush a small post but barely register on a large column. But a stress of 30 MPa means the same thing in both: the material is loaded to a specific fraction of its capacity, regardless of the size of the member. This allows us to test a small specimen in a laboratory and predict the behavior of a full-sized beam or column — an enormous practical advantage. The laboratory does not need to be the size of a building.

Let me impress upon you the importance of area in the calculation of stress. Stress equals force divided by area, so doubling the area halves the stress. This is why a wide foundation settles less than a narrow one under the same load — the contact area with the soil is larger, and the stress on the soil is lower. It is why a thick column carries more load than a thin one. And it is why the African practice of building with wide, heavy bases and tapering walls is structurally sound: the base, which carries the most load, has the largest area and therefore the lowest stress.

### Letter 12: On Elasticity and the Spring That Returns

When you stretch a rubber band and release it, it snaps back to its original shape. When you bend a green twig and release it, it springs straight again. When you load a steel beam and remove the load, the beam returns to its original position. This property — the ability of a material to deform under load and return to its original shape when the load is removed — is called elasticity. It is one of the most important properties in structural engineering, because it means that a structure can carry load repeatedly without permanent damage.

The law of elasticity was discovered by Robert Hooke in 1676 and is called Hooke's law: stress is proportional to strain, provided the strain is small. If you double the stress, you double the strain. If you halve the stress, you halve the strain. The constant of proportionality — the ratio of stress to strain — is called the modulus of elasticity, or Young's modulus, named after Thomas Young, who refined the concept in the early nineteenth century. Young's modulus is a measure of stiffness: how much stress is needed to produce a given strain. Steel has a Young's modulus of about 200,000 MPa — it is very stiff. Concrete has a modulus of about 25,000 MPa — less stiff, but still substantial. Timber has a modulus of about 10,000 to 15,000 MPa along the grain. Rubber has a modulus of only a few MPa — it is very flexible, which is why a rubber band stretches easily while a steel wire barely extends at all.

Hooke's law has a limit. If you stretch the rubber band too far, it will not return to its original shape — it will either remain permanently stretched or snap. The stress at which a material ceases to obey Hooke's law is called the elastic limit. Below the elastic limit, deformation is temporary and reversible. Above it, deformation is permanent and irreversible. For structural steel, the elastic limit is approximately 250 MPa (for common grades). For concrete in compression, it is roughly 40% of the ultimate strength. The structural engineer designs so that stresses remain well below the elastic limit under normal loads, ensuring that the structure always returns to its original shape.

Consider a simple spring. If you hang a 1-kilogram mass from a spring and the spring stretches 10 millimeters, Hooke's law tells you that a 2-kilogram mass will stretch it 20 millimeters, and a 3-kilogram mass will stretch it 30 millimeters. The spring has a stiffness (force per unit extension) that is constant within its elastic range. A structure behaves similarly. A floor beam that deflects 5 millimeters under one person will deflect 10 millimeters under two people and 15 millimeters under three (assuming the beam remains elastic). This predictability is the great gift of elasticity: it makes calculation possible. If materials did not obey Hooke's law, structural engineering would be a matter of guesswork rather than science.

Elastic energy is the energy stored in a deformed elastic body. A compressed spring stores energy; when released, it pushes back. A bent beam stores energy; when the load is removed, it straightens. This stored energy is recoverable — it can do work. But it can also be dangerous. A heavily loaded column, if it suddenly buckles, releases its stored elastic energy violently, and the failure is explosive. A tensioned cable, if it snaps, whips back with lethal force. The builder must respect stored energy, for it can turn a controlled structure into an uncontrolled projectile in an instant.

Why does rubber snap back but clay does not? Because rubber is elastic — its molecular chains uncoil under load and recoil when released. Clay is plastic — its particles slide past each other permanently under load. Most structural materials are elastic up to a point and then become plastic. The engineer's art is to keep the material in its elastic range under service loads, while ensuring that if the structure is overloaded, it deforms plastically (giving warning) rather than fracturing suddenly (giving none). We shall explore this distinction between ductile and brittle failure in the next letter.

### Letter 13: On Yield and Fracture and the Moment of Failure

Every material has a breaking point. The question that matters most to the structural engineer is not whether a material will fail — everything fails eventually — but how it fails, and how much warning it gives before it does.

When a steel bar is pulled in tension, it behaves elastically at first: stress is proportional to strain, and if the load is removed, the bar returns to its original length. But at a certain stress — called the yield stress — something changes. The bar begins to stretch rapidly with little or no increase in stress. The material has yielded. It is no longer elastic; it is plastic. If the load is removed now, the bar will not return to its original length. It has been permanently deformed.

This yield plateau is one of steel's greatest virtues. It means that when a steel structure is overloaded, it does not snap suddenly — it stretches, it bends, it deforms visibly. The occupants have warning. The building sags, doors stick in their frames, cracks appear in plaster. There is time to evacuate, to shore up the structure, to avert catastrophe. This property — the ability to deform significantly before failure — is called ductility, and it is the reason that steel is the preferred material for structures in earthquake zones.

Beyond the yield plateau, steel continues to resist increasing stress through a phenomenon called strain hardening. The stress-strain curve rises again, though less steeply than before. The bar is getting stronger even as it deforms. Eventually, the bar reaches its ultimate tensile strength — the maximum stress it can carry. Beyond this point, the bar begins to neck: a localized reduction in cross-section forms, stress concentrates there, and the bar breaks. The ultimate strength of structural steel is typically 400 to 550 MPa, roughly 1.5 to 2 times the yield stress.

Concrete tells a different story. When a concrete cylinder is compressed, it behaves approximately elastically up to about 40% of its ultimate strength. Beyond this, micro-cracks begin to form and grow. The stress-strain curve bends, becoming less steep. At the ultimate compressive strength (typically 20 to 50 MPa for structural concrete), the cracks coalesce, and the concrete crushes. There is no yield plateau, no ductile deformation, no warning. One moment the concrete is carrying load; the next, it is rubble. This brittleness is concrete's great weakness, and it is the reason we reinforce concrete with steel. The steel provides the ductility that the concrete lacks, and the combination — reinforced concrete — is both strong and forgiving.

Timber fails in a manner that depends on the type of loading and the direction relative to the grain. In tension along the grain, timber fails by brittle fracture — the fibers snap. In compression along the grain, timber can yield by fiber buckling, which provides some ductility. In bending, timber typically fails in tension on the bottom face, because wood is weaker in tension than in compression. The failure is often sudden and audible — a sharp crack. The experienced carpenter learns to listen to the wood, for it speaks before it breaks. A creak or a groan under load is the wood's way of saying it is nearing its limit. The builder who heeds this warning may avert disaster; the builder who ignores it may not be so fortunate.

The structural engineer designs with safety factors precisely because failure is the consequence of miscalculation. A safety factor of 1.5 on steel yield means the structure is designed so that the actual stress is no more than two-thirds of the yield stress. A safety factor of 2.0 on concrete ultimate strength means the actual stress is no more than half the crushing strength. These factors account for variability in materials, uncertainty in loads, imperfections in construction, and the consequences of failure. A footbridge might have a modest safety factor; a hospital or a school, where many lives depend on the structure, will have a larger one. The safety factor is not a sign of ignorance — it is a sign of humility in the face of uncertainty.

### Letter 14: On Wood and the Grain That Guides

Wood is the oldest structural material, and in much of Africa it remains the most accessible. The carpenter who shapes a rafter from a log, the builder who raises a timber-framed house, the farmer who constructs a granary from poles and thatch — all work with wood, and all must reckon with its singular property: the grain.

Wood is an anisotropic material, meaning its properties depend on direction. Along the grain (parallel to the direction of growth), wood is strong and stiff. Across the grain (perpendicular to the direction of growth), it is weaker and more flexible. This is because wood is composed of long, hollow cells — fibers — aligned along the tree's axis. Loading parallel to the grain loads the fibers in their strong direction, like pulling a bundle of straws lengthwise. Loading perpendicular to the grain loads the fibers sideways, like pressing on the bundle from the side — the fibers crush easily.

The practical consequence is that the orientation of the grain relative to the load determines the wood's capacity. A beam must be oriented so that the grain runs along its length, because bending loads create tension and compression along the beam's axis. A post must be oriented so that the grain runs vertically, because the compression load runs vertically. If you cut a beam with the grain running diagonally, it will be far weaker than one with straight grain, because the diagonal grain creates shear planes where the wood can split easily.

African carpenters have long understood which species serve which purpose. Iroko (Milicia excelsa), found across West and East Africa, is prized for its durability, resistance to insects, and strength — it has a bending strength of about 85 MPa and a modulus of elasticity of about 10,000 MPa. African mahogany (Khaya species) is lighter and easier to work, suitable for joinery and furniture but less suitable for heavy structural work. Mvule, teak, African blackwood — each has its character, its strength, its best use. The traditional carpenter's knowledge of species is a form of materials science, accumulated over generations.

Moisture is wood's great adversary. Green (freshly cut) wood has a moisture content that can exceed 50% of its dry weight. As it dries, it shrinks — and it shrinks much more across the grain than along it, which causes warping, splitting, and checking. A beam cut green and loaded immediately may perform adequately at first, but as it dries, it will twist and crack, loosening joints and reducing its effective cross-section. Seasoning — air-drying or kiln-drying timber before use — is therefore essential for quality construction. The moisture content for structural timber should be below 20%, and preferably below 15%.

Wood is also susceptible to biological attack: termites, wood-boring beetles, and fungal decay. In tropical Africa, termites are a particular menace. A termite colony can consume the interior of a structural post, leaving only a thin shell, and the damage may be invisible until the post collapses. Treatment with preservatives, the use of naturally resistant species like iroko, and construction details that keep wood away from direct ground contact are the defenses. The builder who neglects these precautions builds a structure with a hidden expiration date.

Despite these challenges, wood remains an excellent structural material. It is renewable, it is workable with simple tools, it has a high strength-to-weight ratio (comparable to steel in tension along the grain), and it is available throughout the continent. The key is to use it as it wishes to be used — with the grain, dry, protected from moisture and insects, and in species suited to the purpose. The grain is not an obstacle; it is a guide. Follow it, and wood will serve you faithfully.

### Letter 15: On Steel and the Alloy That Builds Civilization

Steel is iron alloyed with a small percentage of carbon — typically 0.1% to 0.3% for structural steel. This modest addition transforms a soft, malleable metal into a material of extraordinary strength and versatility, and it is no exaggeration to say that modern civilization is built upon it. The skeletons of skyscrapers, the cables of suspension bridges, the rails of railways, the reinforcing bars inside concrete — all are steel.

Pure iron is relatively weak. Carbon atoms, which are smaller than iron atoms, wedge themselves into the iron crystal lattice and impede the movement of dislocations — the microscopic defects along which metals deform. The result is a material that is much stronger than pure iron while retaining iron's ductility. The higher the carbon content, the stronger (and harder and more brittle) the steel. Structural steel balances strength with ductility, staying at the low end of the carbon range. High-carbon steel (above 0.5%) is used for tools and springs, where hardness matters more than ductility.

The Bessemer process, invented in 1856, made steel cheap enough for mass production by blowing air through molten iron to burn off excess carbon. Before Bessemer, steel was expensive and used only for weapons and tools. After Bessemer, it became available for construction, and the era of the steel bridge, the steel building, and the steel ship began. Today, steel is produced in electric arc furnaces and basic oxygen furnaces, and global production exceeds 1.8 billion tonnes per year.

The structural properties of steel are remarkable. Its tensile yield strength ranges from 250 MPa (mild steel) to over 500 MPa (high-strength grades). Its Young's modulus is about 200,000 MPa — consistent across all grades, because the modulus depends on the atomic bonds, not the alloy. Steel is equally strong in tension and compression, unlike concrete and wood, which are strong in one direction but weak in the other. This symmetry makes steel ideal for members that must resist both tension and compression — bracing elements, truss members, and columns subject to bending.

In Africa, steel for construction comes primarily in four forms. Reinforcing bar (rebar) is embedded in concrete to carry tension. Structural sections — I-beams, channels, angles, hollow sections — are used for columns, beams, and trusses in steel-framed buildings. Corrugated roofing sheets provide lightweight roof cladding. And wire — plain and galvanized — is used for tying rebar, for fencing, and for cable systems. The Dangote Group, based in Nigeria, now operates a steel plant capable of producing rebar and structural sections, reducing the continent's dependence on imports. Local production lowers cost and improves availability, both of which are critical for African construction.

Steel's weakness is corrosion. In the presence of moisture and oxygen, iron oxidizes — it rusts. Rust is weaker than steel and occupies a larger volume, so it both weakens the member and, when inside concrete, exerts expansive pressure that cracks the surrounding concrete. Protection against corrosion includes painting, galvanizing (coating with zinc), using weathering steel (which forms a protective rust layer), and ensuring adequate concrete cover over rebar. In coastal African cities — Mombasa, Dar es Salaam, Lagos — the salt-laden air accelerates corrosion, and corrosion protection is not optional; it is survival.

### Letter 16: On Concrete and the Stone We Pour

Concrete is an artificial stone made by mixing cement, water, and aggregate (sand and gravel). The cement reacts with the water in a chemical process called hydration, forming crystite crystite crystals that bind the aggregate into a hard, durable mass. It is the most widely used construction material in the world — more than 10 billion tonnes are produced annually — and it dominates African construction for good reason: it is relatively cheap, it can be formed into any shape, and its raw materials are available almost everywhere.

Portland cement, the most common type, is made by heating limestone and clay to about 1450°C in a rotary kiln, producing a clinite called clinker, which is then ground to a fine powder. The Dangote Cement company, the largest cement producer in Africa, operates plants in Nigeria, Ethiopia, Tanzania, and several other countries, with a combined capacity exceeding 50 million tonnes per year. The availability of locally produced cement has been transformative for African construction, reducing both cost and logistics.

The mix proportions of concrete determine its strength. A common structural mix is 1 part cement, 2 parts sand, and 4 parts gravel by volume, with a water-cement ratio of about 0.5. This produces concrete with a compressive strength of approximately 20 to 25 MPa after 28 days of curing. Richer mixes — more cement, less aggregate — produce stronger concrete, up to 50 MPa or more. The water-cement ratio is the single most important factor: less water means stronger concrete, because excess water creates voids when it evaporates, weakening the matrix. But too little water makes the mix unworkable. The builder must balance workability and strength, and this balance is one of the most important skills in concrete construction.

Concrete is magnificent in compression but terrible in tension. Its compressive strength may be 30 MPa, but its tensile strength is only about 3 MPa — roughly one-tenth as much. This means that any part of a concrete structure that is in tension will crack. In a simply supported beam, the bottom face is in tension, and plain concrete will crack there long before the top face crushes in compression. This is why we reinforce concrete with steel bars placed where tension occurs. The steel carries the tension; the concrete carries the compression. Together, they form reinforced concrete — a composite material that is strong in both tension and compression.

The marriage of steel and concrete is one of the great engineering partnerships. Each material compensates for the other's weakness. Concrete protects steel from fire and corrosion. Steel provides the ductility and tensile strength that concrete lacks. Their coefficients of thermal expansion are nearly equal (about 12 x 10⁻⁶ per °C), so they expand and contract together without internal stress. And concrete's alkaline chemistry passivates the steel surface, preventing corrosion as long as the concrete cover is intact and uncracked. It is a partnership that works — provided the builder respects its requirements: adequate cover over the rebar (at least 25 to 50 mm, depending on exposure), proper curing (keeping the concrete moist for at least 7 days after casting), and correct placement of reinforcement (in the tension zone, not randomly).

Curing is perhaps the most neglected aspect of concrete construction in Africa. After the concrete is poured, the hydration reaction continues for weeks, and it requires moisture. If the surface dries out — as it will quickly under the African sun — the reaction stops prematurely, and the concrete at the surface is weak, porous, and prone to cracking. Wetting the surface daily, covering it with wet burlap or plastic sheeting, or applying curing compound are all effective methods. The difference between properly cured concrete and poorly cured concrete can be 30% or more in strength. Thirty percent is the difference between a school that stands for fifty years and one that cracks in five.

## Part IV: Beams and Bending

### Letter 17: On the Beam and the Plank That Spans

The beam is the most common structural element. Every floor is carried by beams. Every roof rests on beams or their curved cousins, arches. Every bridge is, at bottom, a beam or a series of beams. To understand how beams work is to understand the core of structural engineering.

A beam is a structural member that carries loads perpendicular to its length. A plank laid across a ditch to walk upon is a beam. A floor joist carrying the weight of a room is a beam. A bridge girder spanning a river is a beam. In each case, the load acts downward (gravity), and the beam spans horizontally between supports.

There are three fundamental types of beam support. A simply supported beam rests on two supports at its ends, free to rotate at each support. This is the plank across the ditch — if you press on the middle, the ends tilt upward slightly. A cantilever beam is fixed at one end and free at the other — a diving board, a balcony, a bracket shelf. The fixed end must resist both a vertical force and a turning moment. A fixed beam (or encastré beam) is built into its supports at both ends, restrained from rotating at either. Fixed beams are stiffer and stronger than simply supported beams of the same size, because the fixed ends share the bending.

When a load is applied to a beam, the beam bends. The top surface shortens (it is in compression), and the bottom surface lengthens (it is in tension). Between them, there is a surface that neither shortens nor lengthens — the neutral axis. The farther a fiber is from the neutral axis, the greater its stress. This distribution of stress — compression above, tension below, zero in the middle — is the bending stress distribution, and it governs the design of every beam.

Consider a concrete lintel over a window in a house in Kampala. The lintel spans, say, 1.2 meters and carries the weight of the wall above it. The bottom face is in tension. If the lintel is plain concrete, it will crack on the bottom face, because concrete cannot resist significant tension. The builder must place steel reinforcement near the bottom face to carry the tension. This is not a theoretical concern; it is the reason that unreinforced concrete lintels crack and sometimes fall, injuring people below. A reinforced lintel with two 12-mm-diameter rebar near the bottom will carry the load safely for the life of the building. The cost of the rebar is negligible; the cost of neglecting it can be a life.

The span of a beam — the distance between its supports — is the most critical parameter in beam design. As the span increases, the bending moment increases (we shall define this precisely in the next letter), and a larger, deeper beam is needed. This is why long-span structures — sports halls, aircraft hangars, bridge girders — require deep beams or trusses, while short-span structures — house floors, lintels — can use modest members. The span-to-depth ratio is a useful rule of thumb: for a simply supported timber beam, a ratio of about 15 to 20 is typical (a 3-meter span needs a beam about 150 to 200 mm deep). For a reinforced concrete beam, a ratio of about 12 to 15 is common. For a steel beam, 15 to 20. These are starting points, not final designs, but they give the builder an immediate sense of proportion.

### Letter 18: On Bending Moment and the Stress Inside

When a beam bends under load, the internal stresses are not uniform. They vary from maximum compression at the top to maximum tension at the bottom, passing through zero at the neutral axis. The quantity that governs these stresses — that tells us how severely the beam is being bent at any point — is called the bending moment.

The bending moment at any cross-section of a beam is the sum of the moments of all forces on one side of that cross-section about the cross-section itself. This sounds abstract, so let us make it concrete. Consider a simply supported beam of span L carrying a single concentrated load P at its midpoint. At the midpoint, the bending moment is P times L divided by 4, or PL/4. This is the maximum bending moment for this loading arrangement. If P = 10 kN and L = 4 meters, then the maximum bending moment is 10 × 4 / 4 = 10 kN·m (kilonewton-meters).

If the load is uniformly distributed (spread evenly along the beam, as a floor load would be), the maximum bending moment at midspan is wL²/8, where w is the load per unit length. For a beam carrying 5 kN per meter over a span of 6 meters, the maximum moment is 5 × 6² / 8 = 22.5 kN·m. Notice that the moment increases with the square of the span. Double the span, and the moment quadruples. This is why long-span beams are disproportionately larger than short-span ones.

The bending stress at any point in the cross-section is given by the bending formula: stress equals the bending moment times the distance from the neutral axis, divided by the second moment of area (also called the moment of inertia). In symbols, σ = My/I. The maximum stress occurs at the top and bottom of the cross-section, where the distance from the neutral axis (y) is greatest. The second moment of area (I) depends on the shape and dimensions of the cross-section. A deep beam has a large I and therefore low stress for a given moment. A shallow beam has a small I and therefore high stress.

Let me illustrate. A rectangular timber beam 200 mm deep and 50 mm wide has a second moment of area I = bd³/12 = 50 × 200³ / 12 = 33,333,333 mm⁴ (or 33.3 × 10⁶ mm⁴). If the maximum bending moment is 5 kN·m (5 × 10⁶ N·mm), and the maximum distance from the neutral axis is 100 mm (half the depth), then the maximum bending stress is (5 × 10⁶ × 100) / (33.3 × 10⁶) = 15 MPa. If the timber's allowable bending stress is 10 MPa, this beam is overstressed and must be made deeper, wider, or both — or the span must be reduced.

A bending moment diagram is a graph showing how the bending moment varies along the length of the beam. For a simply supported beam with a central point load, the diagram is a triangle: zero at each support, maximum at midspan. For a uniformly distributed load, the diagram is a parabola: zero at each support, maximum at midspan. For a cantilever with a point load at the free end, the diagram is a straight line: zero at the free end, maximum at the fixed end. Drawing the bending moment diagram is the first step in designing a beam, because it tells you where the bending is greatest and therefore where the beam must be strongest.

The significance of bending moment for the African builder is practical and immediate. When you design a floor beam for a school, you calculate the bending moment from the load and the span. You then choose a beam size whose cross-section can resist that moment without exceeding the material's allowable stress. If you are using reinforced concrete, you calculate the area of steel reinforcement needed to carry the tension. If you are using timber, you select a depth and width that keep the bending stress within the timber's capacity. The bending moment is the bridge between the external loads (which you know) and the internal stresses (which you must control). Without it, beam design is guesswork.

### Letter 19: On Deflection and the Sag That Must Be Controlled

A beam that bends under load also deflects — it sags downward (or, in the case of a cantilever, droops). Even if the beam is strong enough to carry the load without breaking, excessive deflection can be a problem. A floor that sags visibly makes the occupants uneasy. A beam that deflects too much can crack the plaster on the ceiling below, damage partitions, or allow water to pond on a flat roof (which adds more load, causing more deflection, causing more ponding — a vicious cycle that has collapsed many a flat roof).

Deflection is calculated from the beam's load, span, modulus of elasticity, and second moment of area. For a simply supported beam with a uniformly distributed load, the maximum deflection at midspan is 5wL⁴/(384EI), where w is the load per unit length, L is the span, E is the Young's modulus, and I is the second moment of area. Notice that deflection depends on the fourth power of the span. Double the span, and the deflection increases by a factor of 16. This is even more dramatic than the bending moment's dependence on span squared, and it explains why deflection, not strength, often governs the design of long-span beams.

The common rule of thumb for acceptable deflection is L/250 for total load or L/360 for live load alone, where L is the span. For a 6-meter span, L/250 is 24 millimeters. Any deflection greater than this is likely to cause cracking of finishes or visible sagging. Some codes are more stringent — L/300 or L/400 — depending on the consequences of deflection.

The L/20 rule is a simpler, rougher guide for timber beams: if the beam's depth is at least L/20 (span divided by 20), deflection will generally be within acceptable limits. For a 4-meter span, L/20 = 200 mm. A 200-mm-deep timber beam spanning 4 meters will likely be adequate for both strength and deflection in a residential floor. This is not a design calculation — it is a starting point, a way for the builder to estimate quickly before refining with detailed analysis.

Deflection can be reduced by increasing the beam's depth (which increases I dramatically, since I depends on the cube of the depth), by increasing the beam's width (which increases I linearly), by using a stiffer material (higher E), or by reducing the span. Of these, increasing the depth is by far the most effective. Doubling the depth of a rectangular beam increases I by a factor of 8, which reduces deflection by a factor of 8. This is why beams are deep and narrow rather than shallow and wide. The material at the top and bottom of the beam, far from the neutral axis, contributes enormously to stiffness; the material near the neutral axis contributes little. Efficiency demands depth.

The African builder constructing a reinforced concrete slab for a second-floor classroom must check deflection as well as strength. A 150-mm-thick slab spanning 4 meters might be strong enough to carry the load without cracking, but it might also deflect 15 or 20 millimeters — enough to crack the plaster below and make the floor feel springy underfoot. Increasing the thickness to 175 or 200 mm might be necessary to control deflection. Alternatively, supporting the slab on secondary beams that reduce the effective span will reduce deflection. The choice between a thicker slab and additional beams is a matter of cost, formwork complexity, and architectural preference — but the underlying physics is the fourth-power dependence of deflection on span.

### Letter 20: On I-Beams and the Shape That Resists Bending

In the previous letters, we established that bending stress is greatest at the top and bottom of a beam and zero at the neutral axis. This distribution has a profound implication for efficient design: material near the neutral axis carries little stress and contributes little to the beam's strength. It is, from a structural standpoint, nearly wasted. The ideal beam cross-section would concentrate material at the top and bottom — where stress is highest — and use as little material as possible in the middle.

This ideal is the I-beam (also called an H-beam or wide-flange section). The I-shape has two horizontal plates (flanges) at the top and bottom, connected by a thin vertical plate (web). The flanges carry the bending stress — compression in the top flange, tension in the bottom flange. The web carries the shear force (the vertical sliding force that also exists in a loaded beam) and keeps the flanges at the correct spacing. The web is thin because shear stresses are modest compared to bending stresses in most beams.

The I-beam is spectacularly efficient. A steel I-beam weighing 30 kilograms per meter might carry the same bending moment as a solid rectangular steel bar weighing 150 kilograms per meter, because the I-beam puts its material where it matters. The second moment of area of an I-beam is many times that of a solid rectangle of the same weight, because the flanges are far from the neutral axis. Recall that I depends on the distance from the neutral axis squared — moving material farther from the center increases I dramatically.

The same principle applies to other materials. A reinforced concrete beam is, in effect, an I-beam in disguise: the concrete in the top carries compression, the steel rebar near the bottom carries tension, and the concrete in between (the "web") carries shear and keeps the top and bottom apart. A timber box beam — two planks top and bottom connected by plywood webs on the sides — applies the same principle in wood. Even a corrugated roofing sheet is an I-beam principle at work: the corrugations create a series of flanges and webs that give the thin sheet far more bending stiffness than a flat sheet of the same thickness.

Why not make the web infinitely thin? Because the web has two jobs beyond carrying shear. First, it must resist buckling. A very thin web, subjected to shear or compression, will buckle (wrinkle like a sheet of paper), losing its ability to carry load. Web stiffeners — vertical plates welded to the web — can prevent this, but they add weight and cost. Second, the web must maintain the spacing between the flanges. If the web fails, the flanges come together, and the beam collapses. So the web must be thick enough to resist buckling and maintain geometry, but no thicker.

The lesson of the I-beam extends beyond beams to all of structural engineering: put the material where the stress is. In a column, the material should be distributed as far from the center as possible to resist buckling — hence the use of hollow sections (tubes) for columns. In a slab, the tension reinforcement should be near the bottom face where tension occurs. In a truss, the top and bottom chords (which carry compression and tension respectively) should be deep apart, connected by web members that carry relatively little force. The I-beam is not just a shape; it is a principle — the principle of structural efficiency, of material used wisely, of engineering that achieves more with less.

### Letter 21: On Sizing a Beam and the Builder's Calculation

Let us now bring together what we have learned about beams and apply it to a practical problem: sizing a floor beam for a two-room school in an African town. The school has classrooms 6 meters wide and 8 meters long. The floor beams span the 6-meter width, spaced 3 meters apart. The floor is a 150-mm reinforced concrete slab. We must size the beams.

First, we establish the loads. The slab weighs 25 kN/m³ × 0.15 m = 3.75 kN/m² (dead load of slab). The beam itself, let us estimate, adds about 3 kN/m (we will check this later). The live load for a classroom is 3.0 kN/m² (students, desks, a teacher). Finishes (screed, tiles) add about 1.5 kN/m². The total load on the slab is 3.75 + 1.5 + 3.0 = 8.25 kN/m². Each beam carries a 3-meter-wide strip of slab, so the load on each beam from the slab is 8.25 × 3 = 24.75 kN/m. Adding the beam's own weight of 3 kN/m gives a total load per meter of approximately 28 kN/m.

We shall apply load factors as required by structural codes: 1.35 for dead load and 1.5 for live load. The factored dead load is (3.75 + 1.5) × 3 × 1.35 + 3 × 1.35 = 21.3 + 4.05 = 25.35 kN/m. The factored live load is 3.0 × 3 × 1.5 = 13.5 kN/m. The total factored load is approximately 38.9 kN/m.

The maximum bending moment for a simply supported beam with uniformly distributed load is wL²/8 = 38.9 × 6² / 8 = 38.9 × 36 / 8 = 175 kN·m. This is the moment the beam must resist.

For a reinforced concrete beam, we size the concrete section first. A rule of thumb gives the beam depth as about L/12 to L/15 for a simply supported beam, so 6000/12 = 500 mm to 6000/15 = 400 mm. Let us try 500 mm deep and 250 mm wide. We then calculate the required steel reinforcement. Using the simplified design approach: the lever arm (distance between the compression center and the tension steel) is approximately 0.85 times the effective depth. With an effective depth of about 450 mm (500 mm minus 50 mm cover), the lever arm is about 380 mm. The required tension force is 175 × 10⁶ / 380 = 460,500 N. If we use steel with a yield strength of 500 MPa (a common grade for rebar), the required steel area is 460,500 / (500 / 1.15) = 1060 mm² (dividing by the material safety factor of 1.15). This is equivalent to four 20-mm-diameter bars (each 314 mm², total 1256 mm²), providing adequate capacity with some margin.

We should check deflection. For a beam of this size and material, the span-to-effective-depth ratio is 6000/450 = 13.3, which is within the typical limit of 20 for a simply supported beam. A more detailed deflection calculation would confirm that the deflection is within L/250 = 24 mm.

This, in essence, is how a beam is designed. The process is: establish loads, calculate bending moment, size the cross-section, determine reinforcement (for concrete) or check bending stress (for timber or steel), and verify deflection. Each step uses the principles we have developed in earlier letters. The arithmetic is not difficult — a secondary school student with a calculator can do it. What matters is understanding each step: why we factor loads, why the moment depends on the square of the span, why the steel goes at the bottom, why deflection must be checked separately from strength. The builder who understands these reasons can adapt to new situations — different spans, different loads, different materials — rather than relying on standard details that may not fit.

## Part V: Columns and Compression

### Letter 22: On the Column and the Pillar That Bears

If the beam is the horizontal workhorse of structures, the column is the vertical one. Columns carry loads downward — from beams, from floors, from roofs — and transmit them to the foundations and the earth. They work in compression, and the question that governs their design is: how will they fail?

A short, thick column fails by crushing. The compressive stress reaches the material's compressive strength, and the material disintegrates. A concrete column, 300 mm square and 1 meter tall, loaded to its ultimate capacity, will explode in a shower of fragments as the concrete crushes. The load at which this happens is simply the compressive strength times the cross-sectional area: if the concrete is 30 MPa and the area is 90,000 mm², the crushing load is 2,700,000 N, or 2,700 kN — roughly 275 tonnes.

But a tall, slender column fails differently. Long before the stress reaches the material's compressive strength, the column bows sideways and collapses. This is buckling, and it is the governing failure mode for most columns in practice. A drinking straw loaded end-to-end will buckle long before the plastic crushes. A bamboo pole used as a scaffold support will buckle long before the bamboo fibers fail in compression. Buckling is a stability failure, not a material failure, and it depends on the column's length, its cross-section, and its material stiffness — not on its material strength.

The distinction between crushing and buckling depends on the column's slenderness — the ratio of its length to a property of its cross-section called the radius of gyration (which is related to the second moment of area). Short, thick columns (low slenderness) crush. Tall, thin columns (high slenderness) buckle. There is a transition zone in between where both effects contribute. The builder must know which regime the column is in, because the design approach is different for each.

Columns appear everywhere in African construction. The concrete columns of a multi-story building in Nairobi. The timber posts of a traditional house in Cameroon. The brick pillars of a veranda in Harare. The steel columns of an industrial warehouse in Johannesburg. In each case, the column must carry its load without crushing or buckling, and the builder must size it accordingly. The traditional African round hut, with its ring of timber poles supporting a conical thatch roof, is a structural system in which each pole acts as a column. The poles are typically short relative to their diameter, so they fail by crushing if overloaded — but since the thatch roof is light, the loads are modest and the poles more than sufficient.

An important practical detail: columns rarely carry purely axial loads. Wind, construction imperfections, and unequal loading from adjacent beams create bending moments in columns. A column subjected to both compression and bending is called a beam-column, and it is more prone to failure than a column carrying pure compression. This is why building codes require that columns be designed for minimum eccentricity — an assumed imperfection that accounts for the inevitable imperfections of real construction. The builder who ignores this, assuming that columns carry only axial load, designs on the unsafe side.

### Letter 23: On Buckling and the Tall Column's Weakness

We arrive now at a subject that holds a special place in these letters, for the formula that governs column buckling was derived by the very author whose voice we have adopted — Leonhard Euler himself, in 1744. Euler's buckling formula is one of the most elegant results in structural engineering, and it tells us something profound about the nature of structural failure.

Euler's formula states that the critical buckling load of a column is P_cr = π²EI/L², where E is the modulus of elasticity, I is the second moment of area of the cross-section, and L is the effective length of the column. The formula tells us that the buckling load depends on stiffness (E and I) and length, but not on strength. A column made of a very strong material will buckle at exactly the same load as a column of the same dimensions made of a weaker material, provided both have the same modulus of elasticity. This is because buckling is a geometric instability, not a material failure. The column does not break; it bows.

Consider a steel column with a circular hollow section: 150 mm outside diameter, 10 mm wall thickness, 4 meters tall. The second moment of area is approximately 10.9 × 10⁶ mm⁴. The modulus of elasticity of steel is 200,000 MPa. Euler's formula gives P_cr = π² × 200,000 × 10.9 × 10⁶ / 4000² = π² × 2.18 × 10¹² / 16 × 10⁶ = approximately 1,343 kN. This is the load at which the column will suddenly bow sideways and collapse, regardless of how strong the steel is.

Now notice what happens if we double the length to 8 meters: P_cr drops to π² × 200,000 × 10.9 × 10⁶ / 8000² = approximately 336 kN. Doubling the length reduces the buckling load to one-quarter. This inverse-square relationship between buckling load and length is the key insight of Euler's formula. It explains why tall columns must be braced, why slender scaffold poles are the most dangerous elements on a construction site, and why the design of tall buildings depends critically on lateral bracing systems (shear walls, cross-bracing, moment frames) that reduce the effective length of columns.

The effective length deserves special attention. Euler's formula uses L, but the effective length depends on the column's end conditions. If both ends are pinned (free to rotate), the effective length equals the actual length. If both ends are fixed (restrained against rotation), the effective length is half the actual length — and the buckling load quadruples. If one end is fixed and the other is free (a flagpole), the effective length is twice the actual length — and the buckling load drops to one-quarter. The lesson is that how a column is connected to the rest of the structure matters enormously. A column built into a rigid floor slab at both ends is four times stronger against buckling than the same column with pinned ends. This is why column-beam connections in reinforced concrete frames are designed to be rigid (moment-resisting) — the rigidity reduces the effective length and increases the buckling capacity.

Bracing is the practical strategy for preventing buckling. A column that is braced at its midpoint has an effective length of half its total length, and its buckling load increases by a factor of four. This is why scaffold poles have cross-bracing at regular intervals, why tall timber posts in traditional construction often have horizontal girts or diagonal braces connecting them, and why steel buildings have bracing systems — X-braces, V-braces, or shear walls — in one or more bays. Bracing is cheap; buckling failure is expensive. The builder who braces generously builds safely.

Let me close this letter with a reflection. Euler derived his buckling formula at the age of 37, already the most prolific mathematician in Europe, soon to lose the sight of one eye (and eventually the other). The formula is a triumph of mathematical analysis applied to a practical problem: why do columns fail? Euler showed that the answer lies not in the material's strength but in the geometry of the column and the stiffness of the material. It is a profoundly counterintuitive result — that a stronger material does not necessarily make a stronger column — and it remains as true today as when Euler wrote it, nearly three centuries ago.

### Letter 24: On Walls and the Vertical Plane

A wall is a column spread wide. Where a column is a point support, a wall is a line support, carrying loads distributed along its length. Walls serve many functions — they enclose space, they resist wind, they provide privacy, they insulate against heat and noise — but their structural function is to carry vertical loads (from roofs, floors, and their own weight) and horizontal loads (from wind and earth pressure) down to the foundations.

A load-bearing wall carries the weight of the structure above it and transmits it to the foundation. In much of Africa, load-bearing masonry walls — built of concrete blocks, fired bricks, or mud bricks — are the primary structural system for low-rise construction. The wall acts in compression, and its design is governed by the same principles as column design: the stress must not exceed the material's compressive strength, and the wall must not buckle.

Wall buckling is a real concern. A tall, thin wall is a slender column, and Euler's formula applies (with appropriate modifications for the wall's width). A common rule of thumb is that the slenderness ratio of a load-bearing wall — the ratio of its height to its thickness — should not exceed about 20 for unreinforced masonry. A 3-meter-high wall should therefore be at least 150 mm thick. If the wall is braced by cross-walls or return walls at regular intervals, the effective height for buckling is reduced, and a thinner wall may suffice. This is why buildings with frequent cross-walls (such as row houses or apartment blocks) can use thinner walls than buildings with large open spans.

The African mud wall deserves special mention, for it embodies engineering wisdom accumulated over millennia. Traditional mud construction in the Sahel and across the continent uses thick walls — often 400 to 600 mm thick — which gives very low slenderness ratios and therefore high resistance to buckling. The walls are often tapered, wider at the base than at the top, which further improves stability. The mud itself is weak in compression — perhaps 1 to 3 MPa — but the walls are thick, so the stress is low. And the round plan form of many traditional huts means that wind load is distributed around the circumference rather than concentrated on a flat face, reducing the bending stress in the wall. The traditional African builder may not know the words "slenderness ratio" or "wind load distribution," but the forms he builds are structurally excellent.

Retaining walls are a special case: walls that hold back earth. The earth behind the wall pushes horizontally, and the wall must resist this push without sliding, overturning, or breaking. The force of the earth depends on the height of the retained soil, the soil's unit weight and friction angle, and the presence of water (which increases the force dramatically). A common formula for earth pressure is the Rankine formula: the horizontal pressure at a depth h is γh times the coefficient of active earth pressure, which is approximately (1 - sin φ) / (1 + sin φ), where γ is the soil's unit weight and φ is the soil's angle of internal friction. For typical soil (γ = 18 kN/m³, φ = 30°), the pressure at 3 meters depth is about 18 × 3 × 0.33 = 18 kN/m². The total force on the wall is the area of the pressure triangle: ½ × 18 × 3 = 27 kN per meter of wall length. This force acts at one-third of the height from the base.

Gravity retaining walls resist this force through their own weight. They are typically massive — thick, heavy walls of concrete or stone. The wall's weight creates a restoring moment about the toe (the front bottom corner), and this moment must exceed the overturning moment caused by the earth pressure. A rule of thumb is that the base width of a gravity retaining wall should be about 0.5 to 0.7 times the retained height. More slender retaining walls use reinforced concrete in an L-shape or T-shape, where the weight of the soil on the base slab helps resist overturning.

### Letter 25: On Foundations and the Earth That Holds Everything

Every force in a structure must ultimately reach the ground. The foundation is the element that makes this transfer, spreading the building's load over enough soil area that the soil can carry it without excessive settlement or failure. The foundation is the last link in the load path, and if it fails, everything above it fails too.

The bearing capacity of soil — the maximum stress it can carry without failing — varies enormously. Soft clay might have a bearing capacity of only 50 kPa (50 kN/m²). Firm clay or loose sand might carry 100 to 200 kPa. Dense sand or gravel can carry 300 to 500 kPa. Solid rock can carry 5,000 kPa or more. The bearing capacity depends on the soil's type, its density, its moisture content, and the depth of the foundation. A geotechnical investigation — boreholes, soil tests, and analysis — is the proper way to determine bearing capacity. But in much of African construction, particularly in rural areas, the builder must rely on simpler methods: probing with a rod, observing the behavior of nearby buildings, and applying conservative estimates.

A spread foundation (also called a pad foundation or isolated footing) is a block of concrete beneath a column, spreading the column's load over a larger area. If a column carries 500 kN and the soil can bear 200 kPa, the required foundation area is 500 / 200 = 2.5 m², which is a square footing about 1.6 m × 1.6 m. The thickness of the footing must be sufficient to resist bending (the soil pushes up on the footing, and the footing spans between the column and its edges) and punching shear (the column tends to punch through the footing like a pin through a sheet).

A strip foundation is a continuous footing beneath a load-bearing wall, spreading the wall's load over a strip of soil. It is the most common foundation type in African residential construction. A typical strip foundation for a single-story house is about 600 mm wide and 200 to 300 mm deep, cast in concrete and sometimes reinforced with a few bars. The width is determined by the wall load and the soil's bearing capacity, using the same principle as for a pad footing: width = load per meter / bearing capacity.

When the soil near the surface is too weak to carry the building's load, the builder must go deeper. Piles — long, slender columns driven or drilled into the ground — carry the load down to a stronger stratum, or develop their capacity through friction along their length in the surrounding soil. Pile foundations are more expensive and require specialized equipment, but they are essential for multi-story buildings on soft ground, for bridges over rivers with deep alluvial deposits, and for structures in areas prone to flooding or erosion.

The depth of a foundation matters for two reasons. First, the soil's bearing capacity generally increases with depth, because the weight of the overlying soil confines the soil below, preventing it from squeezing sideways. Second, shallow foundations are vulnerable to disturbance — by frost heaving (less relevant in tropical Africa), by erosion from rainwater, by burrowing animals, by the shrinkage and swelling of expansive clays. In much of the African savanna, the soil is a black cotton soil (vertisol) that swells dramatically when wet and shrinks when dry. Buildings on shallow foundations in these soils crack as the ground beneath them heaves and subsides with the seasons. The remedy is to found below the zone of seasonal moisture change — typically at least 1.0 to 1.5 meters deep — or to use piles that reach stable ground.

The foundation is unglamorous and invisible, buried in the earth, but it is the most critical element of the structure. A beautiful superstructure on a poor foundation is a disaster waiting to happen. The African builder does well to invest first in understanding the soil, then in building a foundation generous enough to carry the loads with a comfortable margin of safety. What is hidden below the ground determines what can stand above it.

## Part VI: Triangles and Trusses

### Letter 26: On the Triangle and the Only Rigid Polygon

Of all the shapes in geometry, the triangle has a unique structural property: it is the only polygon that cannot change shape without changing the length of its sides. A rectangle made of four bars pinned at the corners can be pushed into a parallelogram — it is a mechanism, not a structure. A pentagon, a hexagon, any polygon with more than three sides and pin joints will collapse when loaded. But a triangle — three bars pinned at three corners — holds its shape rigidly, even though the joints are free to rotate. The bars can only be in tension or compression, not bending, and the shape is stable.

This property makes the triangle the fundamental unit of structural geometry. Whenever a structure must be rigid, the engineer introduces triangles. A rectangular frame is stiffened by adding a diagonal bar, which divides the rectangle into two triangles. A tall tower is stiffened by cross-bracing — diagonal members that triangulate the structure. A roof truss is a network of triangles. A geodesic dome is a sphere made of triangles. The principle is always the same: triangulate for rigidity.

Why does the triangle hold while the rectangle collapses? Because the triangle's geometry fully determines the forces in its members. Given the external loads and the triangle's dimensions, there is one and only one set of internal forces that satisfies equilibrium. The rectangle has too many degrees of freedom — it can deform in ways that the triangle cannot. In the language of mechanics, the triangle is statically determinate: the equations of equilibrium are sufficient to solve for all the forces. The rectangle is a mechanism: it has more degrees of freedom than equations of equilibrium.

Consider a simple example. You are building a gate from three timber bars, pinned at the corners to form a triangle. You can push on any corner with any force, and the gate will not deform (assuming the bars are strong enough). Now build the gate as a rectangle — four bars, four pin joints. Push on one corner, and the entire gate collapses into a parallelogram. Add a diagonal bar (making two triangles), and the gate is rigid again. The diagonal bar has converted a mechanism into a structure, and it has done so by triangulating.

In traditional African construction, triangulation appears in roof structures (the A-frame of a thatched roof is a triangle), in grain stores (the diagonal bracing of an elevated platform), and in fencing (the cross-bracing of a livestock enclosure). The principle is intuitive — builders discover it by trial — but the theory explains why it works and allows extension to new situations. A builder who understands triangulation can stiffen a wobbly scaffold, brace a water tank tower, or design a roof truss from first principles, rather than relying on standard configurations that may not fit the particular situation.

The triangle's rigidity has a corollary: every member of a triangle is either in pure tension or pure compression (assuming loads are applied at the joints). There is no bending. This is an enormous advantage, because materials resist tension and compression far more efficiently than bending. A bar in pure tension uses every fiber of its cross-section equally. A beam in bending uses the top and bottom fibers heavily and the middle fibers hardly at all. This is why trusses — structures made entirely of triangles — can span much greater distances than solid beams of the same weight. The truss converts bending into axial forces (tension and compression) and lets the material work to its full capacity.

### Letter 27: On the Truss and the Frame of Triangles

A truss is a structure composed of bars connected at their ends to form a pattern of triangles. The bars carry only tension or compression — no bending — because the joints are (ideally) pin connections that transmit force but not moment. A truss can span much greater distances than a solid beam of the same weight, because it uses material efficiently: every bar is either pulling (tension) or pushing (compression), and the material is stressed uniformly across its cross-section.

The simplest truss is a triangle of three bars, but practical trusses are more complex. A Pratt truss has vertical members and diagonal members that slope toward the center — the verticals are in compression and the diagonals are in tension. A Howe truss is the reverse: the diagonals slope away from the center, with the verticals in tension and the diagonals in compression. A Warren truss has no verticals at all — only diagonals alternating in direction, each in tension or compression alternately. Each configuration has advantages: the Pratt truss is efficient because its long diagonals are in tension (and tension members can be lighter than compression members, which must resist buckling). The Warren truss is simple to fabricate. The Howe truss was historically popular in timber, because timber is efficient in compression.

The top chord of a truss (the upper horizontal member) is in compression, like the top of a beam. The bottom chord is in tension, like the bottom of a beam. The web members (verticals and diagonals) carry the shear force, transferring load from the top chord to the bottom chord through a zigzag path of alternating tension and compression. The truss is, in effect, an I-beam made of discrete bars: the top chord is the compression flange, the bottom chord is the tension flange, and the web members are the web, arranged in a triangulated pattern.

To analyze a truss — to find the force in each bar — the engineer uses the equations of equilibrium at each joint. At every joint, the sum of horizontal forces must be zero, and the sum of vertical forces must be zero. Starting from a joint where only two unknowns exist, the engineer solves for those forces, then moves to the next joint, and so on until all forces are known. This method, called the method of joints, requires only the ability to resolve forces into horizontal and vertical components and to solve pairs of simultaneous equations. It is accessible to anyone with secondary school mathematics.

The method of sections is an alternative: the engineer imagines cutting the truss through three bars and applies the equations of equilibrium (forces and moments) to one side of the cut. This is particularly useful when the force in a specific bar, deep inside the truss, is needed without analyzing every joint.

Trusses are used extensively in Africa for roof structures, pedestrian bridges, communication towers, and industrial buildings. A steel roof truss spanning 12 meters over a church or a school hall is a common sight. The truss is typically fabricated from steel angles or hollow sections, welded or bolted at the joints, and erected on top of the walls or columns. Its depth (the distance between top and bottom chords) is typically L/8 to L/12, where L is the span. A 12-meter truss might therefore be 1.0 to 1.5 meters deep. This depth allows the truss to carry the roof load with modest forces in its members, keeping member sizes small and costs low.

### Letter 28: On the Roof Truss and the Shelter Over Africa

The roof is the building's first line of defense against rain, sun, and wind. In tropical Africa, where the rains can be torrential and the sun relentless, a well-designed roof is essential. The roof truss is the structural frame that supports the roofing material (corrugated steel sheets, tiles, thatch) and transfers the load to the walls below.

The simplest roof truss is the king post truss: a triangle with a vertical member (the king post) from the apex to the midpoint of the bottom chord. The king post is in tension — it hangs the bottom chord from the apex, preventing the bottom chord from sagging under its own weight and the weight of the ceiling. The rafters (the sloping top chord members) are in compression, carrying the roof load down to the wall plate. The span of a king post truss is typically limited to about 8 meters; beyond this, the member forces become large and a more elaborate truss is needed.

The queen post truss extends the span to about 10-12 meters by adding two vertical members and a horizontal straining beam between them. The Fink truss (or W-truss) uses a W-pattern of web members and can span 10 to 15 meters efficiently. For longer spans — 15 to 30 meters — the Pratt or Warren configurations are used, with the depth of the truss increasing to maintain reasonable member forces.

Let us design a simple king post truss for a community building in a Tanzanian village. The building is 8 meters wide, and the roof pitch is 25 degrees (appropriate for corrugated iron roofing, which requires a minimum pitch of about 10 degrees for adequate drainage). The height of the truss at the apex is 8/2 × tan(25°) = 4 × 0.466 = 1.86 meters. The rafter length is 4 / cos(25°) = 4 / 0.906 = 4.41 meters.

The loads on the roof include the weight of the corrugated iron sheets (about 0.1 kN/m²), the purlins (timber members running perpendicular to the trusses that support the sheets, about 0.15 kN/m²), and the self-weight of the truss (about 0.1 kN/m²). The total dead load is approximately 0.35 kN/m². The live load (for maintenance access) is typically 0.5 kN/m². Wind load is critical for roofs — wind creates suction (uplift) on the leeward side and sometimes on the windward side too. For a basic design in a moderate wind zone, 0.5 kN/m² uplift might be considered.

If the trusses are spaced 3 meters apart, each truss carries a 3-meter-wide strip of roof. The total downward load per unit length along the rafter is (0.35 + 0.5) × 3 = 2.55 kN/m. The total load on each rafter is 2.55 × 4.41 = 11.2 kN. Resolving this into a horizontal thrust at the base (compression in the rafter's horizontal component) and a tension in the bottom chord, we find that the bottom chord tension is approximately the horizontal component of the rafter compression — about 10 kN for this truss.

For timber construction, a king post truss of this span might use 75 × 150 mm rafters, a 75 × 100 mm bottom chord, and a 75 × 75 mm king post. The joints — where the members meet — are the critical details: they must transfer force reliably. Traditional timber joints (mortise and tenon, halved joints) or modern connectors (bolted steel gusset plates, nail plates) can be used. The joint is where most truss failures occur, not in the members themselves. The builder must devote as much attention to the joints as to the members.

A well-built roof truss will last the lifetime of the building — fifty years or more if the timber is durable or treated, the joints are sound, and the roof covering keeps water out. A poorly built truss, with undersized members, weak joints, or untreated timber, may fail in a storm. The difference is not expensive; it is knowledge. And knowledge, once gained, serves every building the builder will ever construct.

### Letter 29: On the Bridge and the Span That Connects

The bridge is among the noblest of structures, for it connects what nature has divided. A river, a gorge, a valley, a road — the bridge spans the gap and carries people and goods across. Africa needs bridges urgently: roads are interrupted by rivers, communities are isolated during the rains, economic activity is throttled by gaps in the transport network. Understanding bridge types and their structural principles enables the builder to choose wisely.

The simplest bridge is the beam bridge: a horizontal member (or several parallel members) spanning between supports on each bank. A log laid across a stream is a beam bridge. A concrete slab spanning a small culvert is a beam bridge. The span of a beam bridge is limited by the bending capacity and deflection of the beam. For timber, practical spans are up to about 10 meters. For reinforced concrete, up to about 20 meters for a simple span. For steel plate girders, up to about 50 meters. Beyond these limits, the beam becomes impractically deep or heavy.

The truss bridge extends the beam bridge's span by using a truss instead of a solid beam. The truss is deeper than a solid beam of the same span (because material is removed from the web and replaced with diagonal members), but it is lighter. Steel truss bridges can span 50 to 150 meters. Many of Africa's older railway bridges are steel truss bridges, built during the colonial era and still in service — a testament to the durability of well-designed steel structures and also a reminder that Africa's infrastructure is aging and needs renewal.

The arch bridge uses a curved member that converts vertical loads into compression along its curve. Arches can span great distances because they work almost entirely in compression, and masonry and concrete are superb in compression. Roman arches, some over 2000 years old, still carry traffic. The African example is the natural arch of rock — formed by erosion, but demonstrating the principle — and the historical masonry arches of Ethiopian and Swahili coast architecture.

The suspension bridge uses cables draped from tower to tower, with the deck hung from the cables by vertical hangers. The cables are in tension, the towers are in compression, and the deck acts as a stiffening element. Suspension bridges can span enormous distances — the longest spans in the world exceed 2 kilometers. For African rivers — the Congo, the Niger, the Zambezi — suspension bridges are sometimes the only practical option when the span is very large and intermediate supports are impossible.

The cable-stayed bridge uses cables running directly from the tower to the deck, in a fan or harp pattern. The deck is stiffer than in a suspension bridge, because the cables are taut and provide rigid support. Cable-stayed bridges are economical for spans of 200 to 500 meters and have become popular worldwide for modern crossings. The Maputo-Catembe bridge in Mozambique, completed in 2018 with a main span of 680 meters, is one of Africa's largest cable-stayed bridges — a symbol of what the continent is building.

The choice of bridge type depends on the span, the loads, the available materials, the soil conditions, and the budget. For a village footbridge over a 10-meter stream, timber beams or a simple steel truss will suffice. For a highway bridge over a 30-meter river, reinforced concrete beams or a steel-concrete composite deck. For a major crossing over a 200-meter gorge, a cable-stayed or arch bridge. For a crossing of 500 meters or more, a suspension bridge. In every case, the structural principles are the same — equilibrium, material capacity, load paths — applied at different scales.

## Part VII: Arches, Domes, and Shells

### Letter 30: On the Arch and the Curve That Carries

The arch is one of the oldest and most elegant structural forms. It carries load primarily in compression, following a curved path from one support to the other. If the shape of the arch matches the shape of the thrust line (the path that the compressive force would naturally follow for a given loading), the arch is in pure compression — no bending, no tension. This is the ideal, and it is why arches built of materials that are strong in compression but weak in tension (stone, brick, concrete) can span great distances and endure for millennia.

The mechanics of the arch are best understood by comparison with the beam. A beam carries load by bending: the top is in compression and the bottom is in tension, and the bending stress increases with the square of the span. An arch carries the same load by compression along its curved axis: there is no tension (if the arch shape is correct) and the compressive stress increases only linearly with the span. This is why arches can span far greater distances than beams of the same material. A stone beam can span perhaps 3 meters before it cracks in tension on its bottom face. A stone arch can span 30 meters or more, because the stone is never in tension.

The price of the arch's efficiency is horizontal thrust. When a vertical load pushes down on the top of an arch, the arch pushes outward at its supports. This horizontal thrust must be resisted — by massive abutments, by tie rods, or by the surrounding structure. If the thrust is not resisted, the arch spreads and collapses. This is why arch bridges have massive stone or concrete abutments at each end, and why arched roofs often have tie rods connecting the bases of the arch to prevent spreading.

The voussoir arch — built of wedge-shaped stones (voussoirs) — is the classic masonry arch. The keystone at the top of the arch is the last stone placed and locks the other voussoirs in position. Each voussoir is in compression, pressed between its neighbors by the thrust of the arch. The mortar between the voussoirs is not structural — it merely fills the gaps. A well-built voussoir arch can carry load even without mortar, as the compression holds the stones in place. This is the principle behind the dry-stone arches found in traditional construction.

The shape of an arch matters critically. For a uniform load, the ideal shape is a parabola. For the arch's own weight alone, the ideal shape is a catenary (the shape of a hanging chain, inverted). For real loading, which is a combination of the arch's weight and applied loads, the ideal shape is somewhere between the two. If the actual loads deviate significantly from the assumed loading (for instance, if a heavy truck parks on one side of an arch bridge), the thrust line moves outside the arch's cross-section, and tension develops. This tension can crack masonry and lead to failure. The arch designer must ensure that the thrust line remains within the arch's thickness for all plausible load combinations.

In African construction, arches appear in doorways and windows (reducing the span that a lintel must carry), in bridges (particularly stone and brick bridges from the colonial and pre-colonial eras), and in vaulted roofs (which we shall discuss shortly). The principles are universal: curve the structure, keep it in compression, resist the horizontal thrust, and match the shape to the loading. The arch is not merely beautiful; it is the most efficient way to span an opening in a compression material.

### Letter 31: On the Dome and the Arch Rotated

A dome is an arch rotated about its vertical axis, forming a three-dimensional shell that encloses space. Like the arch, a dome works primarily in compression, carrying load through its curved surface to the supports at its base. But unlike the arch, a dome has a second structural action: hoop forces — circumferential forces that run around the dome like hoops around a barrel. In the upper portion of a dome, these hoop forces are compressive. In the lower portion, they become tensile. This transition occurs at approximately 52 degrees from the apex for a hemispherical dome under its own weight.

The tensile hoop forces in the lower portion of a dome are the dome's weakness. If the dome is built of a material that cannot resist tension — unreinforced masonry, for instance — the lower portion will crack along meridional lines (vertical cracks running from base to apex). This cracking does not necessarily cause collapse, because the cracked dome can still function as a series of arches, each spanning between two cracks. But it is unsightly and can progress to failure if not restrained. The traditional solution is a tension ring at the base of the dome — a belt of reinforced concrete or steel that encircles the dome and resists the outward thrust, like a hoop around a barrel.

The African round hut — found across the continent from the Maasai manyatta to the Zulu umuzi — is, in its thatched roof, an intuitive dome. The conical thatch roof is not a true dome (it is a cone, which has different mechanics), but the circular plan form distributes load symmetrically, and the ring beam at the top of the wall (where the roof meets the wall) acts as a compression ring that stabilizes the system. The round plan is not merely cultural preference; it is structural wisdom. A round building encloses the maximum area for a given perimeter of wall, uses material efficiently, and distributes wind load evenly.

The rock-hewn churches of Lalibela in northern Ethiopia are among the most remarkable structures in the world. Carved from living basalt in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, these churches were not built up from the ground — they were excavated downward, with the surrounding rock left in place to form the walls, roof, and interior columns. The church of Bete Giyorgis, cruciform in plan, was carved to a depth of over 12 meters. The roof is essentially a dome-like monolithic cap of stone, supported by the walls of the excavation. No mortar, no formwork, no scaffolding — only the removal of stone, with a profound understanding of the material's compressive strength and the geometry that keeps it stable.

Dome construction in modern Africa uses reinforced concrete, which eliminates the tensile cracking problem because the reinforcement carries the hoop tension. The Maputo Cathedral in Mozambique, completed in 1944, features a bold reinforced concrete dome that demonstrates the material's versatility. Modern thin-shell concrete domes can span impressive distances with shells only 50 to 100 mm thick, because the dome shape keeps the concrete in compression and the reinforcement handles any tension. The key to a thin-shell dome is accurate geometry — the shape must be maintained precisely during construction, or the shell will develop bending stresses that the thin section cannot resist.

The dome teaches us that geometry is structure. A flat plate of concrete 50 mm thick can span only a meter or two before it cracks. Curve that same concrete into a dome, and it can span 30 meters. The material is the same; the geometry is different. This is the power of form — the idea that the shape of a structure can do the work that material alone cannot. The arch, the dome, and the shell are all manifestations of this principle, and the African builder who understands it can create structures of grace and efficiency.

### Letter 32: On the Vault and the Extended Arch

A vault is an arch extended in the third dimension — a curved roof that spans a space. The simplest form is the barrel vault: a semicircular (or segmental) arch extended in a straight line, creating a tunnel-like enclosure. Barrel vaults are the natural roof form for rectangular spaces when the builder works in masonry or concrete and wishes to avoid timber beams, which may be scarce, expensive, or vulnerable to termites.

The Nubian vault technique is an African innovation of extraordinary elegance. Developed in upper Egypt and Sudan (ancient Nubia) and still practiced today, it constructs a barrel vault without formwork — without the temporary wooden framework that conventional vault construction requires. The mason builds the vault in a series of leaning arches, each one resting against the previous one. The bricks are thin, light, and laid with a fast-setting mud mortar. Each leaning arch is stable because it leans against its predecessor, and the first arch leans against the end wall. The technique requires only bricks, mud mortar, and the mason's skill — no wood, no steel, no expensive equipment.

This is significant for Africa, where timber for formwork is often scarce and expensive. The Nubian vault technique has been revived and promoted by organizations such as the Nubian Vault Association (Association la Voûte Nubienne), which trains masons across the Sahel — in Burkina Faso, Mali, Senegal, and beyond — to build roofs using this method. The resulting buildings are cool (the thermal mass of the masonry moderates interior temperatures), durable (they resist the termites that destroy timber roofs), and affordable (the materials are local and the formwork cost is zero). It is a case of ancient engineering solving a modern problem.

A groin vault is formed by the intersection of two barrel vaults at right angles. The intersection creates four curved surfaces (groin surfaces) that meet along diagonal ridges (groins). The groin vault is more complex than the barrel vault, but it has a key advantage: the thrusts are concentrated at the four corners, rather than distributed along the walls. This means that the walls between the corners carry no thrust and can be opened up — for windows, doors, or lighter infill. Gothic cathedrals exploited this principle with ribbed vaults, concentrating the thrusts along ribs and allowing the walls between them to be filled with stained glass.

The structural behavior of a vault is similar to an arch: the curved surface carries load in compression, and horizontal thrust develops at the supports. For a barrel vault, the thrust is distributed along the length of the supporting walls. For a groin vault, the thrust is concentrated at the corners. The supporting structure must resist this thrust — through buttresses, tie rods, or massive walls. The Nubian vault technique deals with the thrust by using thick supporting walls (typically 400 mm or more) that can absorb the outward push.

The lesson of the vault, like the lesson of the arch and the dome, is that curvature creates structural efficiency. A flat roof of the same span and material as a vault is heavier, requires formwork, and is weaker. The curve does the work. And the Nubian vault technique demonstrates that this structural efficiency can be achieved with the simplest possible materials and tools, by a trained mason working alone or with one assistant. It is engineering democracy: the knowledge of form made accessible to the ordinary builder.

### Letter 33: On Great Zimbabwe and the Engineering of Stone

Great Zimbabwe is the largest ancient stone structure in sub-Saharan Africa, and it is one of the most remarkable engineering achievements on the continent. Built between the eleventh and fifteenth centuries by the ancestors of the Shona people, the complex covers nearly 800 hectares and includes the Great Enclosure — an elliptical wall 250 meters in circumference, up to 11 meters high and 5 meters thick at its base, constructed entirely of dry-stone masonry without mortar.

Let us consider the engineering of this wall. The granite blocks were quarried from nearby hills and shaped into roughly rectangular forms. They were laid in courses without mortar — each stone resting on the stones below it, held in place by gravity and friction. The wall tapers from 5 meters thick at the base to about 2 meters thick at the top. This taper serves two structural purposes. First, it moves the center of gravity of the wall inward, improving stability against overturning. Second, it reduces the compressive stress at the base, because the wall's weight is greatest at the bottom where the cross-section is largest.

The compressive stress at the base of the wall can be estimated. If the wall averages about 3.5 meters thick and is 11 meters tall, and granite weighs about 26 kN/m³, then the weight per meter of wall length is approximately 26 × 3.5 × 11 = 1,001 kN/m. The base area per meter of wall length is 5 × 1 = 5 m². The compressive stress at the base is therefore about 1,001 / 5 = 200 kPa, or 0.2 MPa. Granite has a compressive strength of over 100 MPa. The wall is stressed to about 0.2% of its capacity in compression. It will never crush; that much is certain.

The question is not crushing but stability. Can the wall overturn, slide, or bulge? Overturning requires a horizontal force — wind or earthquake — to create a moment about the toe that exceeds the restoring moment of the wall's weight. For a wall this massive, the restoring moment is enormous, and only a catastrophic earthquake could threaten overturning. Sliding requires a horizontal force exceeding the friction between the base of the wall and the ground. Again, the wall's enormous weight creates an enormous friction force, and sliding is implausible under any normal loading.

Bulging is the more subtle concern. If the interior of the wall is poorly filled — loose rubble with voids — the outer face can separate and bow outward under the pressure of the rubble core. The builders of Great Zimbabwe addressed this with remarkable care. The outer and inner faces of the wall are built of well-coursed, carefully shaped granite blocks, and the core is densely packed rubble. The coursing of the outer face — the regular horizontal lines of the stone courses — ensures that each stone is supported by the stones below and braced by its neighbors on each side. The wall is not merely a pile of stones; it is a carefully engineered structure in which geometry and friction work together to create stability.

The Great Enclosure has stood for over 500 years, through earthquakes, storms, and centuries of neglect after the city was abandoned. It stands because the engineering is sound: the geometry is stable, the materials are durable, the construction is meticulous, and the design accounts for the forces it must resist. It stands without mortar, without reinforcement, without any technology beyond shaped stone and human understanding.

This is the lesson of Great Zimbabwe for the modern African builder: understanding matters more than technology. The builders of Great Zimbabwe had no concrete, no steel, no cranes, no computers. They had granite, skill, and understanding. They understood that a tapered wall is more stable than a parallel-sided one. They understood that well-coursed stones interlock and resist displacement. They understood that friction between rough stone surfaces creates enormous resistance to sliding. Their understanding was empirical — learned by building and observing — but it was deep and it was correct. The modern builder has better materials and better tools, but the understanding must still be there. Technology without understanding builds poorly; understanding without technology builds Great Zimbabwe.

## Part VIII: Practical Building

### Letter 34: On Load Paths and the Chain from Roof to Earth

Every force in a structure must find its way to the ground. The path that the force follows — from where it arises to where it is finally resisted by the earth — is called the load path. If the load path is continuous, the structure stands. If the load path is interrupted at any point — by a missing connection, a weak member, or an inadequate foundation — the structure fails at that point.

Let us trace a typical load path in a two-story school building. Rain falls on the corrugated iron roof. The rain load (plus the roof's own weight) is carried by the roof sheets to the purlins. The purlins carry the load to the roof trusses. The trusses carry the load to the walls (or columns, if it is a framed building). The walls carry the load, combined with their own weight and the load from the first floor, down to the strip foundations. The foundations spread the load over the soil. The soil resists the load through its bearing capacity. At every link in this chain, the force must be transmitted: from sheet to purlin, from purlin to truss, from truss to wall, from wall to foundation, from foundation to soil.

The connections between links are the critical points. A purlin must be properly fastened to the truss — with bolts, nails, or bearing seats — so that the load is transmitted. If the fastening fails, the purlin separates from the truss, and the roof collapses locally. A truss must bear on the wall with adequate bearing length — typically at least 100 mm for timber on masonry — so that the wall can receive the load. If the bearing length is insufficient, the masonry under the truss crushes, and the truss drops. A wall must rest on the foundation continuously, with no gaps or voids, so that the load is transmitted uniformly to the soil below.

Horizontal forces follow load paths too. Wind pushes on the wall; the wall transmits the force horizontally to the cross-walls or bracing elements; these transmit the force down to the foundations; the foundations transmit the force to the soil through friction and passive earth pressure. If the building has no cross-walls, no bracing, and no moment-resisting frame, the wind load has no horizontal load path, and the building collapses sideways. This is why buildings must be braced in at least two perpendicular horizontal directions.

A common failure in African construction is the interruption of the load path at the wall-foundation junction. If the wall is offset from the foundation — if the foundation is too narrow and the wall overhangs it — the load cannot be transmitted vertically, and the wall cracks and settles. If the foundation is cast and then the wall is built weeks later without cleaning the foundation surface, the bond between wall and foundation may be poor, and horizontal forces (from wind or earth pressure) may separate the two. Continuity of the load path requires continuity of the construction: each element must be properly connected to the element below and the element above.

The builder's discipline is to trace the load path for every significant load — dead load, live load, wind load — and verify that every link in the chain is capable of transmitting the force. This mental exercise, performed before construction begins, reveals weaknesses that might otherwise be discovered only when the structure fails. The load path is not a theoretical abstraction; it is the structure's lifeline, and the builder who traces it builds with intention rather than hope.

### Letter 35: On Dead Load, Live Load, and the Wind That Pushes

A structure must resist several types of load, each with its own characteristics. Understanding these types and how they combine is essential for safe design.

Dead load is the permanent weight of the structure itself: the weight of the concrete, the steel, the masonry, the roofing, the finishes. Dead load is constant — it acts continuously, from the day the structure is completed to the day it is demolished. It can be calculated accurately from the dimensions and unit weights of the materials. Concrete weighs about 24 kN/m³ (25 kN/m³ for reinforced concrete). Steel weighs about 78 kN/m³. Masonry varies from about 12 kN/m³ for lightweight blocks to 22 kN/m³ for dense brick. Timber varies from about 5 to 10 kN/m³ depending on species.

Live load is the variable load imposed by the occupants and contents of the building. It includes people, furniture, stored goods, vehicles, and movable equipment. Live load is specified by building codes as a minimum load per unit area, depending on the building's use. Residences: 1.5 to 2.0 kN/m². Offices: 2.5 to 3.0 kN/m². Classrooms: 3.0 kN/m². Assembly halls: 4.0 to 5.0 kN/m². Warehouses: 5.0 to 10.0 kN/m² or more, depending on what is stored. Balconies and stairs: 3.0 to 5.0 kN/m², because crowds can concentrate there.

Wind load is the force exerted by wind on the building's surfaces. Wind creates pressure on the windward face (the side facing the wind) and suction on the leeward face (the side away from the wind), the sides, and often the roof. Wind load depends on the wind speed (which varies with location, height, and terrain roughness), the building's shape, and the building's size. In a moderate wind zone, the design wind pressure might be 0.5 to 1.0 kN/m² on walls. In a cyclone zone (such as the Mozambican coast or Madagascar), it can exceed 2.0 kN/m².

Wind uplift on roofs is particularly dangerous. A lightweight roof — corrugated iron on timber purlins — can be lifted off the building if the uplift force exceeds the roof's self-weight plus the capacity of the connections. This is why roofs must be tied down to the walls, not merely resting on them. In many African countries, the standard practice of simply laying corrugated sheets on purlins with a few nails is inadequate for storm conditions. Hurricane straps, hold-down bolts, and proper connection details can prevent the catastrophic loss of a roof — which is not merely a structural failure but a humanitarian disaster, since a roofless building offers no shelter.

Earthquake load is an inertial force caused by ground acceleration. When the earth shakes, the building tends to stay still (Newton's first law), and the discrepancy between the moving ground and the stationary building creates forces in the structure. Earthquake forces are proportional to the building's mass (F = ma) and depend on the ground acceleration, the building's natural period of vibration, and the soil conditions. The East African Rift — running through Ethiopia, Kenya, Tanzania, Malawi, and Mozambique — is seismically active, and buildings in this zone must be designed for earthquake loads.

Load combinations are the way these individual loads are combined for design. The structure must be safe under all plausible combinations: dead load alone, dead load plus live load, dead load plus wind load, dead load plus live load plus wind load, and so on. Each load is multiplied by a load factor that accounts for uncertainty. Dead load, being well known, has a modest factor (typically 1.2 to 1.4). Live load, being variable, has a larger factor (typically 1.5 to 1.6). Wind and earthquake loads have their own factors. The structure is designed for the most critical combination — the one that produces the highest stresses or moments.

The builder who understands load types and combinations can make informed decisions about where to invest in a building's structure. The roof needs strong connections (to resist wind uplift). The floor beams need adequate depth (to carry live load without excessive deflection). The columns need adequate cross-section (to carry the accumulated dead and live loads from all floors above). The foundations need adequate area (to spread the total load over the soil). Every element is sized for the loads it must carry, and the loads are determined by the building's purpose, location, and exposure.

### Letter 36: On Reinforced Concrete and the Marriage of Strength

We have discussed concrete's compressive strength and its tensile weakness separately. Now let us examine their union in reinforced concrete — the material that dominates modern African construction — with the care that this critical subject deserves.

When a reinforced concrete beam bends under load, the top half is in compression and the bottom half is in tension. The concrete in the top half carries the compression admirably. The concrete in the bottom half cracks almost immediately (at a tensile stress of only about 3 MPa), and the steel reinforcing bars near the bottom carry the tension. The cracking of the concrete is expected and acceptable — the cracks are fine (less than 0.3 mm wide if the beam is properly designed) and do not compromise the structural capacity, because the steel continues to carry the tension regardless.

The amount of steel needed is determined by the bending moment. The bending moment creates a couple: a compressive force near the top and a tensile force near the bottom. The tensile force equals the compressive force (for equilibrium), and it is carried by the steel. The required area of steel is therefore the tensile force divided by the steel's design strength (yield strength divided by a safety factor, typically 500 / 1.15 = 435 MPa). This is the calculation we performed in Letter 21 for the school beam.

The position of the steel is critical. It must be placed near the face of the member that is in tension. In a simply supported beam, the tension is at the bottom, so the main bars go near the bottom. In a cantilever, the tension is at the top (because the beam bends upward at the support, putting the top in tension), so the main bars go near the top. In a continuous beam (one that spans over several supports), the tension alternates: near the bottom at midspan and near the top over the supports. The reinforcement must follow the tension — a concept called the moment envelope.

Concrete cover — the distance from the steel bar to the nearest concrete surface — is essential for two reasons. First, it protects the steel from corrosion. In a mild environment (interior of a building), 25 mm of cover is adequate. In a moderate environment (exterior, not in contact with soil), 30 to 40 mm. In a harsh environment (coastal, in contact with soil or water), 50 mm or more. If the cover is too thin, moisture and chlorides (from salt or from the soil) reach the steel, it corrodes, the corrosion products swell, and the concrete cover spalls off — a progressive failure that accelerates over time and eventually leaves the steel exposed and the structure compromised.

Second, concrete cover ensures bond between the steel and the concrete. The concrete must grip the steel bar tightly enough to transfer force from one to the other. This bond develops over a length called the anchorage length, which depends on the bar diameter, the concrete strength, and the bond stress. Bars must extend beyond the point where they are needed by at least the anchorage length. If the bars are cut short — a common construction error — they cannot develop their full capacity, and the beam fails at a lower load than expected.

Shear reinforcement — stirrups or links — is also essential. Shear forces in a beam cause diagonal cracking (typically at about 45 degrees), and stirrups crossing these cracks carry the shear force. Without stirrups, a reinforced concrete beam can fail in shear — a sudden, brittle failure with little warning. Stirrups are typically 8 or 10 mm diameter bars, bent into closed rectangular loops and spaced along the beam at intervals of 150 to 300 mm. They are closer together near the supports, where shear forces are highest.

The quality of construction determines whether the designer's intentions are realized. Concrete must be mixed to the correct proportions, placed without segregation, compacted to remove air voids, and cured to develop its full strength. Reinforcement must be clean, correctly positioned, properly lapped where bars are joined, and securely tied to prevent displacement during concrete pouring. These are not optional refinements; they are the essential conditions for the structure to perform as designed. In the African construction industry, where supervision may be limited and workers may lack formal training, these conditions are sometimes not met. The result is structures that are weaker than designed — and the difference may not be apparent until a load event (a full classroom, a storm, an earthquake) exposes the hidden deficiencies.

### Letter 37: On Earthquake Resistance and the Building That Dances

An earthquake is a sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust, producing waves that shake the ground surface. For structures, the critical effect is horizontal ground acceleration — the ground moves sideways, and the building must follow or fail. The East African Rift system, which runs from the Afar Triangle in Ethiopia to Mozambique, is seismically active. Major earthquakes have struck Ethiopia, Tanzania, Malawi, and Mozambique. Any building in this zone must be designed to resist earthquake forces, or it may collapse.

The fundamental principle of earthquake-resistant design is deceptively simple: allow the building to move with the ground rather than resist it rigidly. A perfectly rigid building, if it could exist, would experience enormous forces during an earthquake — the full mass of the building times the ground acceleration. A flexible building, by contrast, "dances" with the ground: it deforms, absorbs energy through its deformation, and experiences lower forces. The key is ductility — the ability to deform significantly without losing strength.

Reinforced concrete frames, properly designed and detailed, are ductile. The steel reinforcement can yield and stretch without the members losing their load-carrying capacity. A well-detailed reinforced concrete building can survive a severe earthquake with damaged beams and columns (cracked, deformed, but still standing) — damage that can be repaired. A poorly detailed building — with insufficient reinforcement, inadequate stirrups, weak connections — collapses, killing its occupants.

The specific detailing requirements for earthquake resistance include: close spacing of stirrups in columns and beams near the joints (to prevent shear failure and confine the concrete); continuous reinforcement through the joints (to maintain the load path); strong columns and weaker beams (so that damage concentrates in the beams, which are easier to repair and less critical for overall stability — the "strong column, weak beam" principle); and adequate anchorage of all reinforcement.

Unreinforced masonry — the dominant construction type in much of rural and semi-urban Africa — is the most vulnerable to earthquakes. Masonry walls are heavy (generating large earthquake forces due to F = ma) and brittle (unable to deform without cracking). When an earthquake shakes an unreinforced masonry building, the walls crack, the roof loses its support, and the building collapses. The remedy is confined masonry: masonry walls framed by reinforced concrete columns and beams at the corners, around openings, and at regular intervals. The concrete frame provides ductility and holds the masonry together even after it cracks. Confined masonry is affordable, uses familiar construction techniques, and dramatically improves earthquake resistance.

Shear walls — reinforced concrete or reinforced masonry walls that are designed to resist horizontal forces — are the simplest and most effective earthquake-resisting elements for low- and mid-rise buildings. A building with shear walls in two perpendicular directions, adequately reinforced and founded on competent soil, can resist moderate earthquakes with minimal damage. The shear walls are stiff (they limit the building's lateral drift, preventing damage to partitions and finishes) and strong (they carry the earthquake forces down to the foundations).

The African builder in the Rift zone cannot afford to ignore earthquakes. The probability of a significant earthquake during the lifetime of a building is not negligible, and the consequences of collapse are catastrophic. The additional cost of earthquake-resistant design — confined masonry instead of unreinforced, closer stirrup spacing, proper detailing at connections — is typically 5 to 15% of the structural cost. This is a modest investment against the risk of total loss.

### Letter 38: On Building Codes and the Rules That Protect

Building codes are documents that specify minimum requirements for the design and construction of buildings. They exist to protect the public: to ensure that buildings are safe, habitable, and durable. They cover structural design (loads, materials, safety factors), fire protection, means of egress, ventilation, plumbing, electrical systems, and much else. Structural building codes specify the loads that buildings must resist, the design methods that may be used, the material properties that may be assumed, and the safety factors that must be applied.

The safety factor is the code's way of accounting for uncertainty. We do not know the exact load that will be placed on a floor (how many people will gather in a room during a celebration?). We do not know the exact strength of the concrete (it varies from batch to batch). We do not know the exact quality of the construction (did the mason leave voids in the wall?). Safety factors — applied to loads (making them larger) and to material strengths (making them smaller) — provide a buffer between what we expect and what might happen. A typical safety factor on live load is 1.5 (the designed capacity is 50% more than the expected load). A typical safety factor on concrete strength is about 1.5 (the design strength is two-thirds of the tested strength). These factors are not arbitrary; they are calibrated through statistical analysis and historical experience to achieve an acceptable probability of failure — typically about 1 in 100,000 per year for structural collapse.

In Africa, the relationship between building codes and actual construction practice is complex. Many African countries have adopted or adapted international building codes (the Eurocode, the British Standards, the American codes, or the South African code SANS 10160). But enforcement is often limited, particularly for small and medium-sized buildings in rapidly growing cities and towns. The formal sector — large commercial and government buildings, designed by engineers and approved by building authorities — generally complies with codes. The informal sector — which constructs the majority of housing in many African cities — often does not.

This does not mean that informal construction is necessarily unsafe. Many informal builders have skills and knowledge passed down through apprenticeship, and their buildings perform adequately for the loads they carry. But informal construction is vulnerable to unusual loads — a heavy crowd on a balcony, a severe storm, an earthquake — because it has not been designed with these loads in mind. The building code exists precisely for these unusual but plausible events. A building that performs well under everyday loads but collapses during a storm has failed its occupants when they needed it most.

The African builder's path forward is not necessarily to hire a licensed engineer for every building (though this is ideal for multi-story and public buildings). It is to understand the principles that codes embody: use adequate safety factors, check load paths, provide bracing against horizontal forces, detail reinforced concrete properly, found the building on competent soil with adequate foundation sizes, and use materials of known quality. These principles are not complicated. They are the principles of these letters, applied systematically. A builder who understands why a code requires 3 kN/m² live load for a classroom, or 25 mm minimum cover on rebar, or stirrups at 200 mm spacing in a beam, will comply not from obedience but from understanding — and understanding is a more reliable guide than obedience, because it adapts to every situation, while rules can only cover the situations their authors foresaw.

The codes are rules that protect. But understanding is the deeper protection. And these letters aim to provide the understanding.

## Part IX: Meditation

### Letter 39: On Euler, Buckling, and the Mathematician Who Understood Columns

We have come nearly to the end of our journey through the strength of things, and it is fitting to pause and reflect on the voice in which these letters have been written. Leonhard Euler — the Swiss mathematician born in Basel in 1707, who lived and worked in St. Petersburg and Berlin, who published more mathematics than any other person in history, who continued to do mathematics even after losing his sight — is the author whose manner we have adopted. And it is no accident that we have done so, for Euler is intimately connected to the subject of these letters.

In 1744, Euler published a treatise on elastic curves — the shapes that a thin, flexible rod takes when compressed. He showed, through mathematical analysis, that a column compressed between its ends will remain straight until the load reaches a critical value, at which point the column will suddenly bow sideways into a curved shape. This critical load — P_cr = π²EI/L² — is Euler's buckling formula, and it is one of the most important results in structural engineering. It tells us that the strength of a column (against buckling) depends on the stiffness of the material (E), the geometry of the cross-section (I), and the length of the column (L) — but not on the material's strength. A column of weak steel and a column of strong steel buckle at the same load, if their dimensions and moduli are the same.

Euler's derivation was a tour de force of mathematical physics. He set up a differential equation governing the deflected shape of the column, solved it using trigonometric functions, and obtained the critical load as a natural consequence of the mathematics. The solution involves the number π — not because circles are involved, but because the governing equation is the same as the equation for a vibrating string, and π appears in both. This mathematical connection between buckling and vibration is profound: a column at its critical buckling load vibrates at zero frequency (it is on the verge of instability), and the mode shape is a half-sine wave — exactly the shape that a vibrating string takes at its fundamental frequency.

Euler understood columns in a way that no one before him had. He understood that buckling is not a material failure but a geometric instability — that the column does not break but bows, and that the load at which it bows depends on its proportions, not its substance. This understanding has saved countless lives, because it tells the engineer exactly how to prevent buckling: increase the stiffness (use a material with higher E), increase the moment of inertia (use a wider or deeper cross-section), reduce the length (brace the column at intermediate points), or fix the ends (change the end conditions to reduce the effective length).

Euler's letters to a German princess — written in the 1760s, when he was already blind and dictating to an assistant — are among the most charming works of scientific exposition ever written. They explain physics, astronomy, philosophy, and mathematics in plain language, with warmth and patience, to a young woman with no technical training. The letters are proof that deep understanding can be communicated simply, and that the barrier between expert and layperson is not intelligence but explanation.

These letters on the strength of things are written in Euler's manner because Euler's manner is the right manner for this subject: patient, clear, rooted in first principles, respectful of the reader's intelligence, and committed to understanding over formula. If the African builder who reads these letters understands why a beam bends, why a column buckles, why a triangle is rigid, and why an arch is strong — even if he never performs a single calculation — he has gained something that no formula alone can provide: the ability to reason about structures, to see forces where others see only walls and beams, and to build with understanding rather than guesswork.

### Letter 40: On the African Builder and the Strength of Understanding

Africa has been building for millennia. The Great Enclosure at Great Zimbabwe, with its 250 meters of dry-stone wall standing 11 meters high without mortar, is older than most European cathedrals. The rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, carved from living basalt to depths exceeding 12 meters, are feats of engineering that the builders of medieval Europe could not have replicated. The Nubian vault technique, using simple bricks and mud mortar to build curved roofs without formwork, is an innovation that modern engineers are rediscovering and promoting across the Sahel. The traditional African round hut, with its circular plan, ring beam, and conical thatch roof, is a structurally optimized form that distributes loads symmetrically and resists wind efficiently.

These are not primitive constructions. They are sophisticated engineering solutions, developed empirically over centuries, tested by use, and refined by observation. The builders who created them understood forces, materials, and geometry — not in the language of Newton and Euler, but in the language of experience and craft. They knew that a tapered wall is more stable than a parallel-sided one. They knew that curved surfaces are stronger than flat ones. They knew that dry-stone construction requires careful fitting and heavy coursing. They knew, in short, the principles of structural engineering, even if they could not have stated them mathematically.

Modern Africa is building at an unprecedented pace. The Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam — one of the largest hydroelectric dams in the world, with a main structure 155 meters high and 1,780 meters long — represents the scale of African ambition. Kigali, rebuilt from the devastation of 1994, is now a model of planned urban development, with well-designed buildings and infrastructure. Lagos, Nairobi, Dar es Salaam, Addis Ababa — these cities are adding millions of square meters of construction every year. The demand for builders who understand structural principles is enormous and growing.

The challenge is to bridge the gap between the African building tradition — which is rich in empirical knowledge but often lacks mathematical formalization — and modern structural engineering — which is powerful in analysis but sometimes disconnected from local materials, techniques, and conditions. These letters attempt to serve as that bridge. They start from first principles (force, equilibrium, material behavior) and build upward to practical applications (beams, columns, trusses, foundations), using African examples throughout. They use mathematics sparingly but honestly, as a tool for precision rather than an obstacle to understanding.

The strength of understanding is this: the builder who understands why a beam bends can design a beam for any span, any load, any material. The builder who merely memorizes a table of standard beam sizes is helpless when the span is unusual, the load is unexpected, or the material is unfamiliar. Understanding scales; memorization does not. Understanding adapts; rules break. Understanding is the difference between a craftsman and an engineer, and Africa needs both — craftsmen who build with skill, and engineers who build with understanding, and ideally, builders who are both.

From the granite walls of Great Zimbabwe to the concrete face of the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, from the mud-brick vaults of the Sahel to the steel towers of Lagos, the African builder has always shaped material to resist force. These letters have aimed to illuminate the conversation between force and material — to make it audible, visible, calculable. The builder who hears this conversation, who sees the forces flowing through beams and columns and arches and foundations, who can calculate whether the material will hold or fail, possesses a strength that no tool can provide and no circumstance can take away. It is the strength of understanding, and it is the strongest thing of all.

## Epilogue: On the Strength That Endures

We have traveled, in these forty letters, from the simplest concept — what a force is — to the design of beams, columns, trusses, arches, and foundations. We have met Newton's three laws, Hooke's law of elasticity, and Euler's buckling formula. We have examined wood, steel, and concrete — their strengths, their weaknesses, and their proper uses. We have traced load paths from roof to earth, sized beams for classrooms, and contemplated the engineering genius of Great Zimbabwe.

The principles we have covered are not merely academic. They are the principles by which every structure stands or falls. A beam bends because the load creates a moment, and the moment creates compression on one face and tension on the other. A column buckles because its slenderness exceeds its material's stiffness, and Euler's formula predicts the critical load. An arch carries load in compression because its curved geometry redirects vertical force along a curved path. A triangle is rigid because its geometry leaves no room for deformation. A foundation must spread the building's load over enough area that the soil can carry it. These are not opinions; they are the laws of nature, discovered by observation and confirmed by mathematics, operating identically in Zurich and in Zaria, in a laboratory and on a construction site.

The structures that endure — the pyramids, the Parthenon, the Great Enclosure, the Lalibela churches — endure because they obey these laws generously. They are over-engineered, in the best sense: their materials and geometry provide far more capacity than the loads demand, leaving ample margin for the unknown. The structures that fail — the collapsed school, the toppled bridge, the cracked apartment building — fail because one or more of these laws was violated, usually at a single critical point: a connection too weak, a beam too shallow, a foundation too narrow, a column too slender.

The strength that endures is not the strength of materials alone. Concrete cracks. Steel corrodes. Timber decays. The strength that endures is the strength of understanding — the builder's knowledge of forces and materials, of load paths and safety factors, of geometry and equilibrium. This understanding, applied with care and honesty, produces structures that shelter families, educate children, bridge rivers, and endure through generations. It is the greatest tool the builder possesses, and it is the gift these letters hope to provide.

Build well. Build with understanding. Build what endures.
